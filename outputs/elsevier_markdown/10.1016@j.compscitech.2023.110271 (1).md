# Polymer nanocomposite adhesives with internal nano-heaters to facilitate multilayer laminate separation

## Body

### Introduction

Almost 20 years after the isolation of single layer graphene from graphite, research on this material is still ongoing due to the wide range of applications derived from its excellent electrical, thermal, optical, and mechanical properties [1–3]. Although the majority of these investigations have been directed towards applications related to perfect monolayers, such as optoelectronics [4], photonics [5], field-effect transistor (FET) sensors [6], etc., one of the fields that has developed most rapidly, leading to tangible applications, is that of polymeric nanocomposites [7–9].

The incorporation of graphene into polymeric materials has led to improvements in the materials mechanical properties [10,11], thermal stability [9], as well as providing new functionalities such as electrical [7,12] or thermal conductivity [13]. In this respect, graphene has been combined with a great variety of polymers, such as, polyolefins (linear low density polyethylene, LLDPE [14]; high density polyethylene, HDPE [15,16]; isotactic polypropylene, iPP [17]), vinyl polymers (poly(vinyl alcohol), PVA [18–20]; poly(vinyl chloride), PVC [21]), acrylics (poly(methyl metacrylate), PMMA [22]), polyesters (polyethylene therephthalate, PET [23]) and elastomers (styrene-ethylene-buthylene-styrene block copolymer, SEBS [24–26]), among others.

Due to its versatility, polyurethanes (PURs) have been used for many years in applications like paints, coatings, sportswear, sound insulation and adhesives. In the latter case, PUR adhesives are the most widely employed for multilayered film materials for pharmaceutical and food packaging, due to the strong and irreversible adhesion that they provide. However, this strong adhesion poses a major problem for the post-consumer treatment of such packaging materials, whose end-of-life scenario is mostly limited to incineration or landfill.

In order to mitigate this important loss of valuable materials and to incentivize their recovery, thermoreversible PUR adhesives incorporating Diels-Alder (DA) molecules, which are able to promote the rupture of the PUR network when heated, have been recently developed [27–30]. Their adhesion properties can be switched on and off by a simple external stimulus, which represents a ground-breaking solution for the recycling of packaging materials. In recent preceding studies we evaluated the incorporation of different DA molecules into solvent-based and solvent-free PUR adhesives and determined the optimal combination. We concluded that the bonding/debonding properties of the adhesives were mainly controlled by the concentration of the DA molecules, with a minimum thermoreversible bond (TRB) content required, which depends on the system and the total ratio of all polyols in the adhesive formulation [27,28].

However, in multilayer materials for packaging, the adhesive is located in the internal layers. Thus, the breakdown process is conditioned by the low thermal conductivities of the outermost polymer layers and, in addition, can be affected by the melting or softening of some polymer components in the multilayer system, which can be antagonistic to the efficiency of the adhesive rupture process.

In this respect, the incorporation of internal ‘nano-scale’ heat generators in the adhesive layer emerges as a very interesting strategy. Graphene exhibits extraordinary thermal conductivity [31], high surface area [32] and strong optical absorption in the near-infrared (NIR) region [33], making it an ideal material to efficiently absorb radiative energy and heat up the surrounding adhesive via a photothermal conversion process [34]. In other words, under external NIR stimuli, the incident infrared photons are absorbed by graphene exiting electrons, which through electron-phonon interactions causes efficient heating of the sp2 graphene sheet. Due to its high thermal conductivity, this heat is then transferred to the surrounding adhesive producing a rapid increment of its temperature [35,36].

Recent studies reported this phenomenon for self-healing [37–39], where graphene assists the repair of a polyurethane network under conventional heating [39] and microwave [38] or IR radiation [37]. The latter example is particularly interesting, where a photothermal effect controls the temperature rise in the polymer network. This approach with graphene would offer several advantages, such as highly localized heating within the adhesive layer, remote triggering, high energy efficiency and increased bond rupture rate.

In a very recent study we have investigated the incorporation of graphene into solvent-based PUR adhesives as a model to test the ability of graphene to increase the rate and efficiency of adhesive rupture under heating by irradiation [40]. Although very promising results were obtained, the presence of an organic solvent in the adhesive systems, in this case, ethyl acetate that assists in the dispersion of graphene, is environmentally undesirable and the use of solvent-free adhesives is preferred. However, the incorporation of graphene into solvent-free systems is much more challenging.

Herein, we report on the preparation of IR-responsive thermoreversible solvent-free adhesives by combining commercial polyurethane with DA molecules and graphene, where the latter absorbs IR radiation and rapidly dissipates the generated heat to the surrounding environment causing the collapse of the adhesive network. Several graphene loadings were evaluated and it was found that a loading of 0.25 wt % of graphene provides the best balance between optical properties and the required photothermal effect, being sufficient to modify the adhesive network after very short irradiation times. It is shown that under these conditions the adhesive films undergo significant changes in both mechanical properties and fluidity. It is observed that these changes become more pronounced as the graphene loading is increased.
### Materials and methods

#### Materials

The DA adduct, designated as DAF, was synthesized by reaction of 1,1′-(methylene-di-4,1-phenylene) bismaleimide (BMIF Sigma-Aldrich, CAS number: 13676-54-5) with furfuryl alcohol (FA) (Sigma-Aldrich, Darmstadt, Germany. CAS number: 98-00-0, 98%), following a procedure recently reported by our group [27].

The solvent-free adhesive system (SF), composed of commercial isocyanate prepolymers and polyols (a mixture of hexanedioic acid, polymer with 2,2-dimethyl-1,3-propanediol and 1,6-hexanediol), was kindly provided by SAPICI SpA, Milan, Italy.

Graphene platelets (GP) of 6–8 nm flake thickness were purchased from Ionic Liquid Technologies (Heilbronn, Germany).

##### IR-sensitive adhesive preparation

Homogenization of the DAF and dispersion of the graphene particles was carried out by mixing (9 min at 2000 rpm) and degassing (9 min at 2200 rpm) polyol (POH), DAF, FA and graphene particles using a planetary centrifugal mixer (PCM) Thinky ARE 250. Subsequently, the isocyanate prepolymer (PNCO) was added to the mixture and mixed for another 6 min at 2000 rpm followed by an additional degassing step of 6 min at 2200 rpm.

The adhesive films were formed by manually spreading the mixtures onto a polyamide sheet to achieve a thin film thickness. This presented several advantages, such as the complete elimination of bubbles, good control of the films thickness and the ability to form films with a relatively large area, all of which are of paramount importance when producing multi-layered materials for food packaging. Finally, the films were cured at 50 °C for 20 h and then left for a further 6 days at RT.

#### Characterization

##### Fourier transform infrared spectra

FT-IR spectra were acquired on a PerkinElmer Spectrum Two FTIR spectrometer with a universal attenuated total reflectance (ATR) sampling accessory. Spectra were recorded accumulating 12 scans at 4 cm−1 spectral resolution. The retro Diels Alder (rDA) conversion was monitored by following the signal intensity of the absorbance band corresponding to the maleimide = C–H out of plane bending vibration [41] that appears near 690 cm−1.
##### Differential scanning calorimetry

DSC analysis was performed on a DSC25 with a RSC90 refrigerated cooling system (TA Instruments). The experiments were carried out in an inert atmosphere on samples of approximately 5 mg placed in hermetically sealed aluminum pans under a nitrogen flow of 50 mL/min. The following thermal cycles were applied: (a) heating from −70 °C to 200 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C/min, (b) isothermal treatment at 200 °C for 3 min, and (c) cooling from 200 °C to 40 °C at a cooling rate of 40 °C/min.
##### Raman spectroscopy

Graphene nanoplatelets were characterized using a Renishaw InVia Reflex Raman microscope. The Raman scattering is excited using a diode laser at a wavelength of 785 nm. The laser beam is focused on the sample with a 100× (0.85 N A.) microscope objective. Raman spectra were collected using an exposure time of 10 s for 10 accumulations.
##### Scanning electron microscopy

GP powder and the morphology of the adhesives composites was investigated using a field emission scanning electron microscope (S-8000/Hitachi). The cross-section of the adhesive samples was analyzed after cryofracture.
##### Real-time monitoring of the photothermal effect

The photothermal effect of the nanoparticles on the adhesives was evaluated by irradiating the thin film samples fixed to a hollow polypropylene holder with an IR-lamp (Philips R125, 250W) and monitoring the surface temperature with a thermal IR-camera (FLIR TG165, area tested = 35 mm2) as a function of time. The distance between the sample and the lamp was set to 6 cm. The camera-sample distance was set to 24 cm. Both lamp and camera were positioned at an angle of approximately 15°.
##### Photothermal reversibility of DA/r-DA reaction

The adhesive film was illuminated with an IR-lamp (Philips R125, 250W) during 90 s, and the reverse Diels-Alder (rDA) reaction conversion was immediately verified via ATR-FTIR and DSC. Subsequently, the sample was placed in an oven at 60 °C overnight. The DA reaction conversion was verified using ATR-FTIR by monitorization of the decrease of the signal intensity at 690 cm−1 and indirectly confirmed by analysis of the enthalpy of the rDA process using DSC.
##### Sample fluidity

Film samples (0.9 × 4.5 cm2) were suspended with a 32 g weight attached to them using bulldog clips. The samples were irradiated with an IR lamp (Philips R125, 250W) at a sample-to-lamp distance of 6 cm and sample deformation was monitored with both video and infrared cameras.
##### Creep test

Creep behaviour was analyzed using a Q800 thermomechanical analyser (TA Instruments) working under tensile mode. Samples with ≈2 mm width, 25 mm length and 150μm thickness were tested. Measurements were carried out at 40 °C with a constant tensile stress of 1 MPa for 180 min. Creep was measured prior and post sample irradiation with an IR lamp (Philips R125, 250W) for 5 min at a distance of 6 cm.

### Results and discussion

The dispersion of graphene in PUR adhesives is not a straightforward process under normal circumstances, but in this case where the PUR adhesive is highly viscous due to it being solvent-free, a good dispersion becomes even more complicated. However, once well-dispersed it has been demonstrated that higher viscosity can be beneficial as it inhibits nanoparticle re-aggregation [42]. The graphene materials selected in this study are powdered nanoplatelets (GP) and Fig. S1 shows SEM images of GP under different magnifications. Analysis of the GP sample over 50 different flakes gives an average size of 12 ± 2 μm x 6 ± 1 μm. The smaller the lateral dimensions of the graphene, the lower the aspect ratio and, therefore, the less efficient the property transfer to/from the polymeric material in nanocomposites. However, in this study these smaller dimensions facilitate good dispersion of the graphene in the solvent-free adhesive. The Raman spectrum of GP (Fig. S2) shows the typical graphene characteristics; the G band, corresponding to the first-order scattering of the E2g mode, at around 1580 cm−1; the disorder-induced D band at 1300 cm−1 and the low-intensity second order 2D band at around 2600 cm−1. However, the ID/IG intensity ratio, related to the level of defects in the 2D laminates clearly displays a very low value of 0.56.

In order to check the dispersion of GP in the commercial PUR adhesives, it was incorporated at a concentration of 0.25 wt % in commercial PUR with a PNCO/POH index of 1.7 and a thin film was prepared (Fig. S3). Optical inspection suggests that the GP-containing films appear transparent with good dispersion throughout the adhesive.

Adhesives containing DAF and GP with loadings of 0, 0.25, 0.5 and 1 wt % (Table 1, entries 2, 3, 4 and 5, respectively) were prepared. Fig. 1 a shows the images of the thin films produced using the PUR control sample containing DAF (SF-A1), and also containing GP at the different loadings (SF-A2 to SF-A4). As expected, it can be clearly seen that as the concentration of GP increases the films become darker and less transparent. The optical properties of the adhesives are important for the aesthetics of the multicomponent system, especially in food packaging where the visualization of the product is frequently required. Notwithstanding, it is important to note that the films in this study display thicknesses ranging from 160 μm to 180 μm (see details in caption of Fig. 1), which are almost 2 orders of magnitude thicker than those of adhesive layers in multilayer plastic laminates. The films shown in Fig. 1a were cryofractured and analyzed using SEM, as shown in Fig. S4. In general, it can be seen that there is a good affinity between the adhesive matrix and the nanofiller; however, at higher loadings (1 wt %) they tend to produce some agglomerates.

Fig. 1b shows thermal imaging of the adhesive films in Fig. 1a on the application of NIR radiation for 90s. It is found that the adhesive films containing graphene present a much higher temperature than the control sample. In addition, the temperature increases with the graphene loading. Preceding studies on PUR adhesives that contain the DAF adduct show that the temperature at which their reversible bonds are opened lies in the temperature range of 90–130 °C [27]. Hence, it appears that 90 s of IR irradiation can trigger bond opening even for the sample with the lowest graphene content. The kinetics of heating under IR irradiation was also evaluated using the photothermal set-up displayed in Fig. S5 and the results are included in Fig. 1c. When the lamp is turned on (t = 0), the temperature in the control sample slightly increases up to ca. 60 °C. Upon addition of graphene, a rapid increase in temperature can be found at very short irradiation times, indicating a significant photothermal effect. In fact, the required temperature to trigger the rupture of DA bond is achieved in all cases in under 30s. As expected, the greater the graphene loading the faster the sample is heated (the initial heating rates are 7.1 °C/s, 8.3 °C/s and 10.4 °C/s for loadings of 0.25, 0.5 and 1 wt%, respectively) and the higher the maximum temperature reached (142 °C, 156 °C and 174 °C, respectively).

After confirming the photothermal effect, we evaluated whether this local heating is capable of triggering the rupture of the thermoreversible bonds in the solvent-free adhesive films. In this system, the thermoreversible bonds are Diels Alder groups that, after heating, revert to BMIF and FA-terminated polyurethane chains. In our previous work, we identified the IR band centered at 690 cm−1, corresponding to the maleimide = C–H out of the plane bending vibration of BMIF, as a useful marker for monitoring the thermoreversible bond rupture in this type of samples [27]. Therefore, the evolution of the intensity of this band was monitored with respect to the irradiation time for samples containing different graphene loadings, and the results are included in Fig. 2 . It can be seen that the band at 690 cm−1 clearly develops under IR irradiation for all graphene-containing samples and this depends on the graphene loading. The sample with the highest graphene content reaches the maximum peak intensity after 30 s of irradiation, while for the samples with lower loadings 60 s are needed to achieve the same intensity. Nevertheless, even in these cases extremely short times are needed to induce the PUR network rupture and this is due to the local hot spots produced on the graphene surface under IR irradiation. In fact, for similar samples heated in an oven at 120 °C, around 15 min are needed to achieve a comparable intensity of the BMIF band [27]. Although the band at 690 cm−1 was used as a reference due to its high intensity, other bands were also used to describe the same behavior with changes in the irradiation time. It can be seen that a band at 720 cm−1, assigned to the asymmetric C–O–C stretching of the heterocyclic rings, disappears, while a new band at 710 cm−1, attributed to the wagging of the FA furan's ring HCCH group [43], develops with the irradiation time.

From the above results it can be concluded that the sample with a graphene loading of 0.25 wt % has a good balance of optical and photothermal properties and is sufficient to induce the rupture of the adhesive network in a short time. Therefore, we focused on samples with this loading for further studies and Fig. 3 shows the cyclability behavior of the SF-A2 sample under several irradiation cycles (Table 1, entry 3). This is a crucial parameter in the recycling of multilayer laminated materials for packaging, since a good network formation/breaking cyclability means that the adhesive can potentially be reused multiple times. The samples were irradiated for 90s and then stored at 60 °C for 20h before testing and repeating this process twice more. Fig. 3a shows the variation of the photothermal effect with irradiation time over three cycles. The temperature versus irradiation time profile for all of these cycles is very similar, with only a slight decrease observed in the maximum temperature. Nevertheless, even after the last cycle the sample could still be heated to 130 °C in less than 60 s, indicating that its photothermal activity is maintained. The cyclability of the DA reaction, i.e. whether the network breakdown/reconstruction process could be repeated over several cycles, was also evaluated by DSC. Fig. 3b includes the DSC heating scans taken immediately after the irradiation cycles (breaking of covalent bonds, blue lines) and after storage at 60 °C for 24 h (network reconstruction, red lines). The DSC heating scan of the control PUR samples shows an endothermic peak ranging from 90 °C to 150 °C, which corresponds to the breakdown of the thermoreversible DA bonds [27,44,45]. Here, it can be observed that the DSC scans collected immediately after irradiation only present a slight change in the slope around the thermoreversible bonds rupture temperature range, suggesting that most of the thermoreversible bonds have been broken by the IR treatment. On the contrary, the DSC cycles recorded after thermal annealing at 60 °C for 24 h display a very similar profile to that of the control sample, suggesting good cyclability. Infrared studies on these samples also display a similar trend, where it is seen that the FTIR band at 690 cm−1 related to the BMIF is absent in the control sample, but appears after irradiation, and again disappears following a 24 h annealing process at 60 °C (Fig. 3c). From the integration of the DSC peak, the degree of network reconstruction after irradiation and posterior annealing can be estimated, with the results displayed in Fig. 3d. The enthalpy for each rupture/reconstruction cycle decreases after the first heating scan, but then remains constant upon the application of subsequent thermal treatments. These results indicate that after the first rDA reaction the DA structure is not completely recovered during the isothermal treatment at 60 °C. Similar results were reported for a solvent-based PUR system with thermoreversible DA adducts without graphene, where the network rupture was triggered by conventional heating. However, in the same study it was observed that for the solvent-free adhesive the enthalpy progressively decreased with heating cycles, indicating a gradual loss in the network reconstruction efficiency [27]. Here the combination of IR radiation and graphene lead to a more efficient and stable network breakdown/reconstruction, which is crucial for materials recycling.

The next step of this study consisted in selecting the best adhesive formulation in terms of its polyol/isocyanate ratio, concentration of thermoreversible bonds and content of FA, according to how its physical properties were altered under the effects of the IR irradiation. The rupture of thermoreversible bonds is expected to produce a significant decrease in the average molecular weight between crosslinks of the adhesive giving rise to important macroscopic changes in its physical properties that ultimately facilitate its reuse. The expected decrease in molecular weight should have a repercussion on the flowability of the prepared samples. Hence, the elongation under irradiation of a series of samples with different formulations and the same graphene loading was evaluated. In these experiments the samples were suspended and irradiated with a weight clamped to their bottom edge. Samples containing 0.25 wt % of GP, PNCO/POH ratios of 1.3 (SF-A2 and SF-B2) and 1.7 (SF–C2; SF-D2) and FA contents ranging from 1.6 to 2.4 wt % were compared to their respective control samples without graphene (SF-A1; SF-B1; SF-C1 and SF-D1) and the results shown in Fig. 4 . The red and grey columns in Fig. 4a represent the change in the length of the samples without and with 0.25 wt % graphene, respectively after 2 min of irradiation. The neat SF adhesives show an elongation ≤15%, except for the sample SF-A1, which shows an elongation approaching ≈40%. It is noteworthy that this sample contains the highest amount of FA. Preceding work on solvent-based thermoreversible adhesives showed that FA aided the flow of the polyurethane, which was associated to a higher free network volume arising from the FA acting as plasticizer and chain stopper [28]. From Fig. 4a it can be seen that the elongation of SF-B1, SF-C1 and SF-D1 are all similar, however upon the incorporation of graphene there is a significant increase in the elongation values, with the largest increase seen for sample SF-D2. It is noteworthy that this sample exhibits one of the highest TRB contents (11.5%) together with the smallest quantity of POH in the formulation (see Table 1). On the one hand, large quantities of TRB involve abundant chain scissions arising from the rDA process and this would deteriorate the resistance to mechanical flow. In fact, results reported on conventionally heated polyurethane networks containing DA bonds suggest that there is a TRB concentration threshold that must be surpassed in order to induce changes in the physical properties of the adhesives [40]. On the other hand, the SF-D2 adhesive exhibits the lowest POH quantity in the formulation (see Table 1) and hence, the highest average molecular weight between permanent crosslinks, as discussed above. In this situation, lower crosslinking density means higher free volume, which is beneficial in terms of mechanical flow upon bond breakage. Fig. 4b shows photographs and thermal images taken during the irradiation of a control sample (SF-D1) and the sample containing 0.25 wt% of graphene (SF-D2) after 2 min irradiation. It can be observed that, while the reference sample shows little variation in the overall sample elongation, the graphene-containing adhesive shows an important change in length. This is also evidenced by the thermal images (bottom of Fig. 4b) that show a higher temperature and homogeneous heat distribution in the graphene-containing sample.

From the above results, it can be seen that the sample SF-D2 is the most sensitive to radiation and, consequently, we have proceeded to study its mechanical properties. The flow properties of the SF-D2 sample were further assessed by creep testing prior to and post irradiation. Tensile creep behaviour was measured and the results are shown in Fig. 5 for the control SF sample, the DAF-containing sample without (SF-D1) and with 0.25 wt% addition of graphene (SF-D2). Measurements were carried out before and immediately after IR irradiation at 40 °C, a temperature above the glass transition of all samples and below the rDA temperature (see Fig. 3b). Error bars are included and represent the standard deviation associated with the average value of three independent tests. In the first place, it is found that the mechanical behaviour at 40 °C prior to irradiation significantly changes with the incorporation of the thermoreversible bonds. Indeed, SF exhibits a marked elastic behaviour associated to a sudden strain increase followed by a levelling-off at short times. Fig. 5 shows that the introduction of DA bonds significantly changes the network elastic response by promoting viscoelasticity. In this case, strain increases at a much slower pace and the rate of increase stabilises after ≈90 min approaching a steady-state regime. Further addition of graphene does not appear to significantly influence the flow properties at 40 °C, neither in terms of the total creep extent nor related to the creep rate in the steady state regime. Most interesting are the results immediately after IR irradiation. The neat SF sample exhibits little change in its creep properties, and the differences are within the limits of error. More significant is the creep enhancement after IR illumination of the SF-D1 sample. This increase may be associated with an enhanced network thermal expansion with respect to that of the neat SF adhesive. As commented above, the lower quantity of POH used in the formulation of the new adhesives materials with respect to starting SF is expected to promote a network structure with lower average crosslinking density and higher free volume. In turn, this should enhance thermal expansion. However, most important is the large creep increase observed after irradiation of the sample containing 0.25 wt % graphene, with respect to that without graphene. Such a significant difference can be associated to the absorption of radiation by graphene that distributes the heat in such a way that the temperature inside the sample rises well above that of the r-DA reaction (see Fig. 1c). Consequently, abundant bond breaking takes place and the mechanical properties of the samples change significantly.

The cyclability of the selected SF-D2 sample was also investigated by DSC and FTIR. In a previous study we found that the network recovery in thermo-reversible solvent-based polyurethane adhesives depends on the treatment applied to the samples after heating. We observed that storing the samples at room temperature for a longer period of time resulted in a higher percentage of network recovery than annealing at 60 °C for shorter time [27]. This is a very important feature from a sustainability point of view, since lowering the temperature of post-irradiation treatment leads to significantly lower energy consumption. Consequently, the effect of post-irradiation sample treatment on the physicochemical properties of SF-D2 was investigated and the results are shown in Fig. 6 . The sample was subjected to different post-irradiation treatments: an irradiation cycle of 90s was applied; then, the sample was annealed at 60 °C for a short period (2 h) and subsequently stored at room temperature for 3 days. This process was repeated twice (cycles 1 and 2). In the third cycle, the sample was irradiated for a further 90 s and annealed at 60 °C for 20 h. Two main features can be highlighted from the DSC curves. Firstly, the shape of the DSC curves of SF-D2 after the first two cycles of irradiation/post-treatment is different to that of the control sample, in that the broad endothermic peak splits into two well-defined maxima after storage at room temperature. These two peaks correspond to the unlocking of the diastereomers of the thermoreversible DA moieties [46]; the maximum at lower temperature is attributed to the endo diastereomer, while the process at higher temperature corresponds to the exo diastereomer [46,47]. A higher proportion of endo diastereomer is usually achieved under kinetic control of the reaction, while exo products are thermodynamically more favorable [47]. The ratio between endo and exo isomers has been studied as a function of the chemical nature of the substituent on each side of the DA bond and a variety of results have been observed [48]. In this study, the different cycles are applied to the same adhesive formulations, and thus the changes are not related to the chemical nature of the adhesive, but rather to the breaking/reconstruction processes themselves. The control sample, prepared by curing at 50 °C and the sample annealed at 60 °C after the 3rd irradiation cycle, present a broad DSC endotherm with a maximum at 132–134 °C with a predominant contribution of the exo form, suggesting that at the curing or annealing temperature the thermodynamically more stable diastereomer is favored. On the other hand, the sample stored at room temperature (cycles 1 and 2) shows an important contribution of the less stable diastereomer with two maxima at 108 and 130–135 °C, corresponding to the endo and exo forms, respectively. Favoring the selective formation of the endo form is very relevant from the point of view of sustainability, since obtaining a product rich in this diastereomer would lower the temperature at which the thermoreversible bonds would break, leading to important energy and cost savings.

The integration of the DSC peak suggests that the initial enthalpy of the process (control sample ΔH = 9.5 J g−1) is maintained after the annealing post-treatment (cycle 3, ΔH = 9.4 J g−1), whereas a slight decrease is observed for the samples held at room temperature after irradiation (cycle 1, ΔH = 7.3 J g−1; cycle 2, ΔH = 8.7 J g−1). This suggests that the degree of network reconstruction after irradiation is more favored at slightly higher temperatures than at room temperature. This is also a very relevant aspect for sustainability as the decrease in the network reconstruction limits the times that an adhesive can be reused. Therefore, there appears to be a trade-off between the energy used to break the thermoreversible bonds and the efficiency of the network reconstruction for reuse.
### Conclusions

Novel adhesives that incorporate remote triggering of adhesion loss are described. These adhesives contain small amounts of graphene that under IR irradiation acts as an internal heater promoting the rupture of the adhesive network. It is demonstrated that the adhesive with a graphene content of 0.25 wt % and a polyol/isocyanate ratio of 1.7 exhibits remarkable changes in its flow and creep properties after irradiation.

The results of this study open the door to the preparation of reversible adhesives where adhesion can be controlled on demand via short irradiation cycles. This is of paramount importance in applications such as the recycling/recovery of multilayered materials used in packaging. In these materials the adhesive is located in the inner layers. The graphene located in the adhesive layer is sensitive to electromagnetic radiation and generates local ‘hot spots’ via the photothermal effect, increasing the rate and efficiency of the network breakup. This approach offers several advantages, including highly localized heating within the adhesive layer, external triggering, high energy efficiency, increased bond rupture rate and short timescale. It is also an attractive, simple and scalable strategy to be able to integrate it into recycling lines. For instance, IR irradiation over a short time period would facilitate the separation of the individual polymer layers from the multilayer films to produce clean waste streams for effective recycling, and thus move away from a linear model of “take-make-dispose” towards a circular economy model for problematic multilayer packaging.
