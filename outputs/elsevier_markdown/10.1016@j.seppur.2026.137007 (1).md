# Bridging porosity and performance: design rules for industrially viable metal organic frameworks in CO₂ capture

## Body

### Introduction: the paradigm shift from equilibrium to process-relevant design

#### The prevailing focus on equilibrium metrics

For the past two decades, MOFs research for CO₂ capture has been focused on record-breaking equilibrium properties such as maximum CO₂ uptake capacity (mmol/g), high CO₂/N₂ selectivity, and moderate adsorption enthalpies (Qst). While these metrics provide valuable thermodynamic insights, but real processes involve kinetics, heat transfer and cycling. [1] [2] [3] [4]. A MOF exhibiting exceptional adsorption capacity may show slow kinetics, poor stability under humid conditions, or high synthesis costs, makes it unsuitable for industrial deployment [5]. The field has inadvertently reinforced this equilibrium-centric view by: (i) Reporting single-point isotherms at idealized conditions (dry gas, 298 K, 1 bar). (ii) Neglecting dynamic breakthrough curves and cyclic capacity under realistic feeds [6]. (iii) Underemphasizing capital cost implications of slow kinetics (larger beds, longer cycles) [7] [8]. (iv)Treating material stability as a binary "stable/unstable" classification without mechanistic insight [9].
#### Why process-relevant metrics matter

Productivity (kg CO₂ captured per kg adsorbent per hour) is the most critical metric for determining equipment size and capital cost. For the same throughput, high-capacity but slow uptake MOFs require larger beds than fast-kinetics MOFs in TSA, cycle times limited by heat transfer increase adsorbent requirements and CAPEX [10] [11]. Mass and heat transfer limitations dominate real-world performance. Intraparticle diffusion through micropores (typical time constant: seconds to minutes) Film resistance at particle surfaces (seconds) Macropore transport in shaped pellets/beads (seconds to minutes) Thermal wave propagation in TSA beds (minutes to hours) [10] [12] [13]. Capital costs are often CAPEX dominated for TSA (large beds, heat exchangers) and compressor dominated for VSA/PSA [1].

To systematically review CO₂ capture materials in the context of these process-relevant metrics, we adopt a structured framework that organizes materials and processes along three complementary axes: application scenario (post-combustion, pre-combustion, or direct air capture), process type (TSA, VSA, PSA, ESA, or hybrid cycles), and validation level (laboratory powders, shaped pellets, modules, or pilot-scale systems). This framework provides a clear context for evaluating performance metrics such as productivity, stability, energy efficiency, and economic viability, and it underpins the presentation of representative materials and processes in Tables 3 and 4. By explicitly linking material properties to process conditions and validation stage, the review connects laboratory and simulation-level insights to potential pathways for practical deployment. Benchmark MOFs for CO₂ capture include Mg-MOF-74 for high capacity and selectivity, UiO-66 for exceptional stability, UTSA-16 for competitive adsorption under humidity, and CALF-20 for hydrophobicity and thermal robustness, providing a basis for comparing performance across TSA, PSA, and other separation processes.
#### Derivation of the five process-informed design rule: systematic evidence-based approach

The five process-informed design rules proposed in this review are derived from a systematic synthesis of prior literature rather than experiential judgment, drawing on a meta-analysis of 397 peer-reviewed studies (2020–2025) encompassing process simulations, techno-economic analyses, and pilot-scale demonstrations of adsorption-based CO₂ separation systems. The cited studies are used to synthesize recurring, process-level trends reported across literature rather than to introduce standalone results from individual works [14] [1] [15] [16] [17] [18]. Across these studies, productivity and kinetics consistently emerge as dominant CAPEX drivers due to their impact on bed size and cycle time, while long-term cyclic stability and material lifetime govern OPEX through replacement frequency and performance degradation under humid and impurity-containing conditions [13] [19]. High-throughput computational screenings that integrate molecular simulation with process optimization further demonstrate that equilibrium metrics such as adsorption capacity and selectivity alone are weak predictors of cyclic performance and economic viability [20] [21] [11]. These conclusions are corroborated by pilot-scale demonstrations, which show that materials exhibiting excellent equilibrium behavior frequently fail to meet productivity, stability, or scalability requirements under realistic operating conditions. Together, this evidence identifies productivity, stability, scalability, material cost, energy efficiency, and economic viability as the key factors underlying the five design rules ( Table 1.1 ). ### Design rule 1: productivity and kinetics—the forgotten metrics

#### Defining productivity in cyclic adsorption

Productivity quantifies the rate of CO₂ capture per unit mass of adsorbent:

Productivity = (kg CO₂ captured) / (kg adsorbent × cycle time)

Typical units: mol CO₂ / (kg adsorbent per hour) or kg CO₂ / (m3 bed per hour)

For a fixed plant throughput (e.g., 1 million tons CO₂/year), low productivity demands proportionally larger adsorbent inventories, increasing both CAPEX (vessels, piping, land) and OPEX (adsorbent replacement, maintenance) [22]. Bed utilization factor (fraction of bed actively participating in adsorption at any instant) is often <50% in TSA due to thermal wave propagation, further inflating required bed volume [23] [24] [25] [26].
#### Mass transfer pathways and limitations

CO₂ molecules must traverse multiple resistances from bulk gas to adsorption site: (i) External film resistance (gas boundary layer at particle surface): ∼1–10 s time constant (ii) Macropore diffusion (in shaped pellets/beads): ∼10–100 s (iii) Micropore diffusion (within MOF crystals) ∼10–1000 s [27] [28]. (iv) Surface barrier (pore mouth constriction): can dominate if pore apertures are small Hierarchical porosity (macro- + meso- + micropores) reduces diffusion path lengths and accelerates uptake [29] [30]. For example, MOFs grown on open-porous fiber substrates exhibited 2–3× faster breakthrough times compared to packed powder beds due to reduced intraparticle diffusion distances. Representive metrics: CPO-27-Ni (spray-granulated 300–700 μm spheres) lead to breakthrough time ≈5 min in lab-scale fixed bed (1% CO₂/N₂, 1 bar, 298 K) [31] whereas CALF-20 (structured pellets) shows Cyclic VSA capacity ≈2.5 mmol/g with 90% recovery in 10-min cycles (dry feed) [32], Mg₂(dobpdc)-fiber composite: 50% uptake time <3 min (400 ppm CO₂, humid air) vs. >10 min for powder [33]. The amine-appended Mg2(dobpdc) fiber sorbents selectively capture CO2 with productivities over 4 mmol/g of the fiber, under humid flue gas conditions, at least five times higher than those of state-of-the-art amine-functionalized silica-based fiber sorbents [11] [34] [35].

From a process perspective, bed volume is inversely proportional to productivity: Bed Volume ∝ 1 Cyclic Capacity × Cycle Frequency

Thus, slow-kinetics MOFs require larger bed volumes, increasing CAPEX and associated infrastructure. For example, a 400 MW NGCC post-combustion CO₂ capture plant (90% capture) using a slow-kinetics MOF would need 3,000 tons of adsorbent versus 1,500 tons for a fast-kinetics MOF, raising CAPEX by ∼$15 million (assuming $50/kg MOF and $10 million for additional vessels/piping) [36] [11].
#### Integration with swing adsorption processes:

Among the various CO2 capture methods, Swing Adsorption Processes selectively separate CO2 and other gases close to room temperature, thus offer a more energy-efficient alternative to conventional techniques, especially for specialized industrial applications [37]. These processes have gained significant attention because the theoretical minimum energy required to recover CO₂ from flue gas and compress it to 150 bar is only about 0.75 GJ/tonne-CO2 [38].

Depending on the methods used for regeneration of adsorbents after each adsorption cycle in a CO2 capture process, several processes can be adopted to achieve CO2 separation, including (1) vacuum and pressure swing adsorption (VSA and PSA), (2) temperature swing adsorption (TSA), (3) electric swing adsorption (ESA), (4) simulated moving bed (SMB), (5) purge displacement ( Fig. 1 ) [39] and (6) Electric/magnetic induction swing adsorption (ESA/MISA) ( Fig. 4 ). In addition to these parameters, economic and technical factors must also be considered during the selection of an adsorption method. Choosing the best adsorption method for purification is thus a significant challenge [40].

CO2 recovery and purity are key parameters that determine the performance of swing adsorption processes [41]. Optimizing both is essential for achieving high process productivity, economic viability, and long-term sustainability. CO2 recovery refers to the percentage of CO2 captured from the feed gas. Higher recovery maximizes the use of the adsorbent and improves energy and process efficiency. CO2 purity indicates how free the captured CO2 is from impurities such as N2, CH4, and moisture. However, there is often a trade-off between recovery and purity. Achieving high purity can reduce recovery or increase energy demand, as more stringent separation conditions may either capture less CO2 or require additional energy for sorbent regeneration. Balancing these parameters is critical in designing efficient and cost-effective CO2 capture systems. To match the transportation/injection requirements, the CO2 recovery must be at 90% with purity higher than 95% [42]. Furthermore, a trade-off exists between the process economics and operational flexibility, which must be carefully balanced to enable the industrial adoption of CO2 capture technologies [43]. One way to introduce flexibility is by relaxing the CO2 recovery constraint; however, this can reduce overall capture efficiency. Adsorption-based processes present a promising solution, as they can be tailored to improve operational flexibility while maintaining high performance within the limits of CO2 recovery and purity [44]. CO2 recovery and purity are calculated using the following equations. (1) CO 2 Purity % = n CO 2 , EVAC n CO 2 , EVAC + n N 2 , EVAC . 100 % (2) CO 2 Recovery % = n CO 2 , EVAC n CO 2 , PRES + n N 2 , ADS . 100 % where n CO2 and n N2 are the number of moles of CO2 and N2 collected in a specific step within one cycle. Note that n CO2, PRES will be zero in the recovery calculation for the four-step cycle with feed pressurization. ADS = Adsorption; EVAC = Evacuation; PRES = Pressurization [45].

The performance of various swing adsorption processes in terms of CO2 purity and recovery is summarized in Table 3 . Among the CO2 adsorption processes evaluated, ESA exhibits the highest CO2 recovery, whereas the hybrid VPSA and PTSA configurations deliver the highest purity, reaching 99%.

To develop efficient CO2 adsorption processes, the adsorbent should exhibit the following key properties: (1) high CO2 selectivity, (2) high CO2 adsorption capacity, (3) use of low-cost raw materials, (4) fast adsorption/desorption kinetics, (5) low heat capacity, and (6) strong thermal, mechanical and chemical stability under prolonged operations and exposure to impurities. A wide range of solid adsorbents have been designed and synthesized with controlled structures, compositions, adsorption mechanisms, and regeneration behavior to enhance CO2 capture performance [46] [47]. Among these, MOFs have emerged as one of the most promising materials in the CO2 capture field, owing to their exceptional chemical and structural tunability, high porosity, water stability, and ease of regeneration MOFs offer a versatile and efficient platform for swing adsorption processes due to their higher CO2 adsorption capacities and selectivity compared to conventional solid adsorbents. When tested at elevated pressures, MOFs have demonstrated higher CO2 adsorption capacities than commercial Zeolite 13X [48]. While a wide range of MOFs have been computationally designed and screened for CO2 capture, experimental validation of their performance under practical process conditions remains relatively limited in the literature. In the following sections, the CO2 capture performance of various MOFs, particularly with respect to key metrics such as CO2 recovery and purity, is evaluated in both individual and hybrid swing adsorption processes. ##### Pressure swing adsorption process (PSA):

PSA is a promising gas capture and separation technique known for its relatively low operational cost, primarily due to its use of moderate pressures. However, the mechanical energy required for the desorption step can offset some of these cost advantages. Moreover, the purity of the recovered adsorbate is often lower compared to other separation methods, which may limit its applicability in processes requiring high-purity outputs. In a PSA process, adsorbents are regenerated by reducing the pressure, making it particularly suitable for separating weakly adsorbed substances, especially when high product purity is required or when rapid cycling enhances the efficiency of the adsorbent use. Extensive computational studies on MOF synthesis and screening have been conducted to design advanced materials aimed at improving the efficiency of CO2 capture and separation processes [49]. The selection of an appropriate adsorbent for CO2 capture depends on the CO2 partial pressure and its source, such as flue gas or ambient air. For instance, zeolites can adsorb large amounts of CO2, but they typically require very low temperatures or vacuum conditions for effective desorption [50] [51] [24]. In contrast, activated carbons are promising candidates for CO2 capture, as they have already been successfully employed in PSA units for CO2 removal during hydrogen purification [52]. Despite its relatively low selectivity and density, the cost of activated carbon remains a critical factor in its evaluation as an adsorbent material. Conversely, MOFs offer high specific gas capture selectivity and capacity, along with the potential for cost-effective production. MOFs consist of inorganic metal nodes coordinated with organic linkers, forming highly tunable structures. This structural versatility allows the design of thousands of distinct frameworks with varying affinities and selectivities for CO2. The pore limiting diameter (PLD) is a critical structural parameter of an adsorbent that governs its selectivity for specific gas adsorbate. If the PLD is smaller than the kinetic diameter of the target gas molecules, those molecules are unable to enter the pores, thereby preventing adsorption [53]. The PLD of MOFs can be readily tuned through a metal substitution, providing an effective strategy to tailor their selectivity for CO2 [54]. Phase-change MOFs represent an emerging class of adsorbents for efficient gas separation, characterized by step-shaped adsorption isotherms and offering both high selectivity and capacity under optimum conditions [55]. A wide range of MOFs have been applied in PSA processes for various gas-related applications, including carbon capture, oxygen generation, hydrogen and methane storage, as well as alkane separation [56].

Grande et al. measured adsorption breakthrough curves for a quaternary gas mixture representative of the composition produced during the steam methane reforming (SMR) process using UTSA-16 MOF as adsorbent (100 g), to mimic real industrial systems [57]. This quaternary mixture is composed of 76% H2, 17% CO2, 3% CH4 and 4% CO simulating the off-gas composition from a typical steam methane reformer. UTSA-16 demonstrated higher selectivity for CO2 compared to the other gases, accompanied by significant heat generation during adsorption, as evidenced by a column temperature rise of 35 K to 55 K at low and high flow rates, respectively. Additionally, UTSA-16 exhibited a higher adsorption capacity for CO2 than zeolites, indicating its potential for use in processes involving the simultaneous production of H2 and CO2.

The previous studies revealed that the adsorption properties of MOFs are regulated by both the enthalpy of adsorption and free volume [58] [59]. Among the four MOFs studied UiO-66(Zr), UiO-66(Zr)-Br, UiO-67(Zr), and Zr–Cl2AzoBDC, UiO-66(Zr)-Br exhibited the highest enthalpies of adsorption for CO, N2, CH4, and CO2. This is attributed to its narrow pores and pore windows, as well as the presence of bromine functional groups, which enhance electrostatic and dispersion interactions, respectively ( Fig. 2 ) [60] [61]. At high pressures, UiO-67(Zr) showed the highest adsorption capacities for all four gases. This performance is likely due to its slightly shorter linkers, which create narrower pores and promote stronger dispersion interactions. Conversely, at low pressures, MOFs with smaller pore sizes, such as UiO-66(Zr) (6.6 Å) and UiO-66(Zr)-Br (6.4 Å), demonstrated the highest uptakes for CO, N2, CH4, and CO2.

Bao et al. investigated a copper-based MOF, Cu(hfipbb)(H2hfipbb)0.5, for the kinetic separation of CO2 and CH4 via a PSA process. The separation leverages the difference in diffusion rates within the MOF’s microporous channels, where CO2 diffuses more rapidly than CH4 due to its smaller kinetic diameter. This MOF exhibited a kinetic selectivity of 26, significantly outperforming conventional adsorbents in CO2/CH4 separation [62]. Mg-MOF-74 is one of the most extensively studied adsorbents for CO2 capture as it shows high adsorption performance even in the presence of moisture, due to its open metal sites located in hexagonal channels that are aligned along the c axis [63]. A computational study using a one-dimensional mathematical model evaluated the post-combustion CO2 capture performance of Mg-MOF-74 in a PSA process [64]. The model incorporated key process parameters, including pressure drop, multicomponent mass diffusion, and mass and heat transfer. A heat regeneration step was also integrated to improve CO2 sequestration efficiency. Under simulated conditions with heat regeneration, Mg-MOF-74 achieved a CO2 purity of 99.65% and a recovery rate of 69.50%. Notably, its capture performance remained stable over 10 adsorption-desorption cycles. Capturing CO₂ before combustion requires isolating CO₂ from hydrogen-heavy gas mixtures generated after the water-gas shift reaction in techniques like gasification or steam-methane reforming. Due to the elevated operational pressures and heightened CO₂ partial pressures, solvent-based absorption systems, including Selexol, are the most advanced towards commercial implementation; nevertheless, the application of solid adsorbents within pressure swing adsorption (PSA) processes is currently receiving considerable scholarly attention [65] [66]. Typical shifted syngas streams contain 15–35 vol% CO₂ and 30–80 vol% H₂ and are produced at pressures commonly exceeding 5–40 bar, conditions that strongly favor pressure-driven separation processes [67] [68]. Because PSA-based hydrogen purification is already widely deployed, pre-combustion CO₂ capture would require only minor modifications to existing processes rather than a complete redesign [69]. Moreover, the massive scale of hydrogen purification (∼50 million tonnes per year) implies that even modest efficiency gains could have substantial energy and economic impact. For instance, reducing the energy consumption of purification (7.3 MW h per tonne H₂) by 10% would save energy equivalent to shutting down 18 average-sized coal-fired power plants [69]. Consequently, the development of next-generation adsorbents for pre-combustion CO₂ capture with improved energy efficiency could significantly lower the environmental and economic costs associated with global H₂ production. Moreover, PSA is particularly advantageous under pre-combustion scenarios because the regeneration of adsorbents can be accomplished through depressurization to near-atmospheric pressure, which is energetically more favorable compared to temperature- or vacuum-swing methodologies [70]. Furthermore, CO₂/H₂ separations are inherently more advantageous than CO₂/N₂ separations, attributable to the greater disparities in polarizability and quadrupole moment, which facilitate enhanced selectivity in physisorption-based systems. In this context, metal–organic frameworks have emerged as promising next-generation adsorbents, exhibiting high CO₂ working capacities and tunable CO₂/H₂ selectivity of 5–8 mmol/g under PSA-relevant high-pressure conditions, although challenges related to stability, impurities, and large-scale deployment remain. Examples encompass magnesium based MOF-74 (Mg₂(dobdc)), nickel-based MOF-74 (Ni₂(dobdc)), copper-based Cu-BTTri MOF [BTTri= (1,3,5-tris(1H-1,2,3-triazol-5-yl)benzene (ligand))], and HKUST-1 (also referred to as Cu₃(BTC)₂), all of which feature accessible metal sites that facilitate robust CO₂ adsorption, in addition to zirconium-based UiO-66-NH₂, recognized for its hydrothermal resilience and moderate affinity for CO₂ [71] [72] [73] [74] [75]. Considering the extensive industrial utilization of Pressure Swing Adsorption (PSA) for hydrogen purification accounting for approximately 50 million tonnes of hydrogen annually even incremental enhancements in adsorbent efficacy could substantially mitigate energy expenditures and environmental repercussions [69].
##### Vacuum swing adsorption process (VSA):

A typical VSA process is commonly designed as a 4-step cycle with feed pressurization, especially in applications like CO2 capture or O2/N2 generation from air [76]. Fig. 3 depicts the four-step VSA process, which includes: (1) adsorption, (2) forward blowdown, (3) reverse evacuation, and (4) feed pressurization. VSA is appealing because it operates at lower pressures and requires less energy than alternative adsorption processes.

Farooq et al. developed a novel two-step methodology for the rapid screening of adsorbents for post-combustion CO2 capture using a four-step VSA process with light product pressurization [77]. Five key equilibrium isotherm characteristics of CO2 isotherm such as binary CO2 loading, CO2/N2 selectivity, Henry’s constant, nonlinearity at feed concentration and local slope were identified that most strongly influence process performance. By applying central composite design and neural network modeling, the authors optimized VSA cycle operating conditions for each combination of isotherm parameters. Additionally, they developed meta-models to estimate the minimum energy consumption and maximum productivity, while also assessing whether adsorbents could achieve the target performance metrics of 95% CO2 purity and 90% recovery. However, most studies primarily rely on breakthrough experiments coupled with numerical simulations to assess separation performance. This limitation stems from the practical challenges associated with scaling up MOF synthesis to the tens-of-grams scale, which is often unfeasible in typical academic settings. An additional limitation is the significant time and resource demands associated with the current VSA process. Rajendran et al. employed a hydrophobic MOF, CALF-20, for CO2 capture from a N2/CO2 gas mixture (85/15 mol%) using an integrated adsorption configuration combining basic VSA with light-product pressurization (LPP) [44]. The model predictions were validated through experimental demonstration of a VSA separation process, guided by a Pareto curve. The experiments showed that the basic four-step VSA cycle achieved 95% CO2 purity with 70% recovery, while the four-step cycle with light-product pressurization (LPP) improved performance to 95.5% purity and 88.1% recovery. Five diamine-doped MOFs, including mmen-Mn2(dobpdc) and mmen-Mg2(dobpdc), which exhibit S-shaped CO2 adsorption isotherms, were evaluated for CO2 capture using the VSA process [78]. A multi-objective genetic algorithm was employed in a comprehensive optimization and simulation study to enhance productivity and reduce parasitic energy consumption. The parasitic energy required per tonne of CO2 captured was lower for the MOFs mmen-Mn2(dobpdc) (142 kWhe) and mmen-Mg₂(dobpdc) (152 kWhe) compared to Zeolite 13X (162 kWhe). Additionally, the CO2 productivities of mmen-Mn2(dobpdc), mmen-Mg2(dobpdc), and Zeolite 13X were 0.40, 0.45, and 0.65 mol CO2 m-3 ads-1, respectively, with CO2 recovery and purity levels of ≥90% and ≥95%.
##### Temperature swing adsorption process (TSA)/ Temperature–vacuum swing adsorption (TVSA):

The TSA process involves the adsorption of CO2 molecules onto a solid adsorbent at a lower temperature, followed by desorption through heating. A typical TSA cycle consists of three main steps: (1) adsorption at ambient temperature and pressure, (2) countercurrent regeneration at elevated temperature, and (3) purging during the cooling phase. The TSA process is widely used for CO2 capture due to its readily available low- and medium-grade thermal energy resources required for complete adsorbent regeneration, as well as its compatibility with renewable energy integration [79].

In a four step TSA cycle, the CO2 capture potential of recently developed MOFs, including MOF-Mg, MOF-Mn, MOF-Co, MOF-Zn, and MOF-Fe was evaluated through a comprehensive modeling study. By optimizing the operating conditions, it was found that the energy requirement for the TSA process using these MOFs, particularly MOF-Mn, was reduced by more than 20% compared to commercial 13X zeolite. Additionally, a smaller temperature swing was sufficient to achieve comparable CO2 purity and recovery levels [80].

Among MOFs, MOF-74 is one of the most widely studied and commercially available materials, known for its high CO2 adsorption capacity and excellent CO2/N2 selectivity. These properties make it one of the most promising adsorbents for efficient and quantitative CO2 capture [81]. Ben-Mansour et al. developed a computational model integrated with Ansys-Fluent and validated both 2D and 3D CFD simulations using TSA CO2 adsorption breakthrough experiments. Under flue gas conditions, Mg-MOF-74 demonstrated a CO2 recovery of 86.5% and a purity of 96.22%. The process achieved a CO2 capture productivity of 0.279 kg of CO2 per hour per kilogram of Mg-MOF-74 and a specific energy consumption of 663.8 kWh per ton of CO2 captured [82]. MOFs generally exhibit high water uptake under normal capture conditions and water competes with other gases in the flue gas [83]. To address this issue, a techno-economic analysis of MOF-based adsorption cycles for post-combustion CO2 capture from wet flue gas was conducted to assess the cost-effectiveness of using MOFs in TSA processes [84]. The study also compares a single-stage TSA process with a two-stage system that includes flue gas drying followed by a VSA process on dry flue gas. Results showed that the two-stage process offers lower minimum capture costs for near-saturated flue gas at 25 °C and 40 °C. Additionally, indirect regeneration in the TSA system is performed by circulating a heating medium (such as steam or hot fluid) through heat exchanger tubes embedded within the adsorbent bed. Peh et al. reported that the MOF UTSA-16, operated in a five-step TSA cycle, achieved a maximum CO2 productivity of 91.8 kg CO2 m-3 MOF h-1 when treating a wet flue gas feed at 25 °C containing 15% CO2, 3% H2O (95% relative humidity), and 82% N2 [85]. A novel Mg2(dobpdc)(tetramine) MOF (Mg2MOF) was used to capture CO2 from dry flue gas (4 mol% CO2) released from natural gas power plant using a three-step steam-assisted TSA (SA-TSA) process [86]. Mg2MOF exhibited a representative double-stepped isotherm achieving more than 95% CO2 purity and over 90% CO2 recovery with the lowest specific steam consumption of 45 kg steam per kg CO2 captured and a maximum CO2 capture productivity of 0.1 molCO2m-3 adsorbents-1 under isothermal conditions. Mg₂(dobpdc)-diamine frameworks (e.g., mmen-Mg₂(dobpdc)) display step-shaped CO₂ adsorption isotherms and high working capacities at low CO₂ concentrations, making them particularly well suited for DAC [87]. For MOF-based direct air capture (DAC), hybrid temperature–vacuum swing adsorption (TVSA) is generally preferred over standalone TSA or VSA [88] [89]. Pure VSA is ineffective at atmospheric pressure because the ultra-low CO₂ concentration in air (∼400 ppm) results in negligible working capacity, even for high-affinity MOFs. TSA alone enables regeneration of amine-functionalized MOFs but suffers from long heating and cooling times and high sensible heat penalties. In contrast, TVSA combines moderate heating with vacuum, significantly lowering regeneration energy and improving working capacity for MOFs such as mmen-Mg₂(dobpdc) and PEI-impregnated MOFs, making it the most widely studied and practically viable DAC process for MOF sorbents [87] [90] [91].
##### Hybrid/integrated swing adsorption process

Pressure-vacuum swing adsorption (PVSA) operates under adiabatic conditions, where temperature changes during pressurization or evacuation can significantly affect the cyclic performance. The system’s response to pressure fluctuations is closely linked to thermal effects, which can influence adsorption efficiency and recovery. PVSA offers advantages over absorption and membrane separation processes due to its lower energy requirements, higher efficiency, and avoidance of corrosive materials. Additionally, compared to TSA, PVSA provides higher productivity and enables faster cycling, making it well-suited for large-scale gas separation applications [92].

A recent study evaluated the CO2 capture potential of a "phase-change" MOF, F4_MIL-140A(Ce), in a post combustion PVSA process [54]. F4_MIL-140A(Ce) performance is attributed to its non-hysteretic, step-shaped isotherm, low uptake for N2, and mild heat of CO2 adsorption, which was found to be better than “canonical” sorbents such as zeolite 13X, activated carbon and three MOFs (i.e. HKUST-1, CALF-20 and UTSA-16).

A modular VPSA lab-scale pilot (comprising 3 columns of 1.1 L each) was developed to evaluate 60 kg of MIL-160(Al) MOF as adsorbent for CO2 capture in various process configurations. The efficiency of MIL-160(Al) MOF for CO2 capture was assessed using a 3-bed 6-step VPSA cycle to separate 15/85 vol% CO2/N2 mixture with a pressure range of 0.1 and 2 bar. The results were compared with zeolite 13X, which showed superior performance of MIL-160(AI), achieving 92.7% CO2 recovery and 90% purity compared to zeolite 13X that yielded 85% recovery and 79.7% purity [93].

To enhance the efficiency of CO2 capture from dry flue gas, a two-stage VPSA process was implemented. The first stage employed conventional zeolite 13X, while the second stage utilized a novel MOF, Mg-MOF-74 [94]. The integrated VPSA system achieved CO2 purity of 97.57% and a recovery rate of 90.16%. Additionally, it demonstrated a productivity of 3.09 mol CO2/kgads/h, with a total energy consumption of 0.7 MJ/kgCO2. Poly vinyl alcohol binded UiO-66 (PVA@UiO-66) MOF revealed selective CO2 adsorption over N2 and the high CO2 separation efficiency in a PVSA process with feed pressure of 1.3 bar [95]. Adsorption time was found to be directly proportional to the CO2 purity that increases from 28% to 34%, while CO2 recovery decreases from 66% to 55%. The increase in adsorption time causes small N2 zone and larger CO2 rich saturation zone therefore exhibit increase in CO2 purity. The pelletization of PVA@UiO-66 MOF caused 14% drop in CO2 adsorption capacity, which was due to the increase in adsorbent’s bull density. The productivity was measured to be 0.077 kgCO2/(kgads·h), which is comparable to the experimentally studied Zeolite 13X efficiency and low operational cost.
##### Electric/magnetic induction swing adsorption (ESA/MISA)

Unlike the TSA process, the ESA process utilizes the Joule effect for low voltage electric current to directly heat the adsorbent for regeneration. As a result, it does not rely on external or waste heat for regeneration, as is typically the case in TSA [101]. Furthermore, ESA process exhibits higher tendency for volatile (i.e., low boiling) organic compounds removal than TSA.

Like TSA process, a typical ESA process has three steps: 1) adsorption at ambient temperature; 2) regeneration via temperature increase using electric heating; 3) cooling (purging) by employing treated gas or nitrogen ( Fig. 4 ). Although MOFs are generally electrical insulators, recent years have seen increasing interest in understanding and tuning their electronic structures, leading to notable improvements in electrical conductivity [102] [103]. However, there are still limited reports in the literature on the application of MOFs for CO2 capture using ESA. Kongi et al. were the first to use a redox-active MOF, Cu3(HHTP)2, as an electrosorbent for CO2 capture, demonstrating its ability to reversibly adsorb and desorb CO2 in an aqueous electrolyte through redox switching [104]. Based on the electrosorption mechanism, it was concluded that the combination of the aromatic ligand and redox-active copper centers in Cu3(HHTP)2 facilitated efficient CO2 capture. The MOF exhibited a CO2 adsorption capacity of 2 mmol/g and an enthalpy of adsorption of 20 kJ mol-1, highlighting its potential as a high-performance CO2 electrosorbent. MOFs exhibit high CO2 adsorption capacities; however, their thermally insulating nature often leads to longer regeneration times and higher energy requirements. To address this challenge, Hill et al. reported a composite of three important constituents, namely MOF (Mg-MOF-74), magnetic nanoparticles (MgFe2O4) and a hydrophobic porous polymer, which can be employed in the magnetic induction swing adsorption (MISA) process [105]. The resulting MOF composite can be regenerated after CO2 adsorption process at low energy cost of 1.29 MJ kg−1 CO2 and high productivity of 3.13 kgCO2 h−1 kgAds−1. The regeneration energy of cost of this MOF is 45% lower than the commercially used adsorbents. Thus, the MISA process has the potential to regenerate adsorbents at significantly higher speeds using a magnetic field, while offering high energy.

### Defining productivity in cyclic adsorption

Productivity quantifies the rate of CO₂ capture per unit mass of adsorbent:

Productivity = (kg CO₂ captured) / (kg adsorbent × cycle time)

Typical units: mol CO₂ / (kg adsorbent per hour) or kg CO₂ / (m3 bed per hour)

For a fixed plant throughput (e.g., 1 million tons CO₂/year), low productivity demands proportionally larger adsorbent inventories, increasing both CAPEX (vessels, piping, land) and OPEX (adsorbent replacement, maintenance) [22]. Bed utilization factor (fraction of bed actively participating in adsorption at any instant) is often <50% in TSA due to thermal wave propagation, further inflating required bed volume [23] [24] [25] [26].
### Mass transfer pathways and limitations

CO₂ molecules must traverse multiple resistances from bulk gas to adsorption site: (i) External film resistance (gas boundary layer at particle surface): ∼1–10 s time constant (ii) Macropore diffusion (in shaped pellets/beads): ∼10–100 s (iii) Micropore diffusion (within MOF crystals) ∼10–1000 s [27] [28]. (iv) Surface barrier (pore mouth constriction): can dominate if pore apertures are small Hierarchical porosity (macro- + meso- + micropores) reduces diffusion path lengths and accelerates uptake [29] [30]. For example, MOFs grown on open-porous fiber substrates exhibited 2–3× faster breakthrough times compared to packed powder beds due to reduced intraparticle diffusion distances. Representive metrics: CPO-27-Ni (spray-granulated 300–700 μm spheres) lead to breakthrough time ≈5 min in lab-scale fixed bed (1% CO₂/N₂, 1 bar, 298 K) [31] whereas CALF-20 (structured pellets) shows Cyclic VSA capacity ≈2.5 mmol/g with 90% recovery in 10-min cycles (dry feed) [32], Mg₂(dobpdc)-fiber composite: 50% uptake time <3 min (400 ppm CO₂, humid air) vs. >10 min for powder [33]. The amine-appended Mg2(dobpdc) fiber sorbents selectively capture CO2 with productivities over 4 mmol/g of the fiber, under humid flue gas conditions, at least five times higher than those of state-of-the-art amine-functionalized silica-based fiber sorbents [11] [34] [35].

From a process perspective, bed volume is inversely proportional to productivity: Bed Volume ∝ 1 Cyclic Capacity × Cycle Frequency

Thus, slow-kinetics MOFs require larger bed volumes, increasing CAPEX and associated infrastructure. For example, a 400 MW NGCC post-combustion CO₂ capture plant (90% capture) using a slow-kinetics MOF would need 3,000 tons of adsorbent versus 1,500 tons for a fast-kinetics MOF, raising CAPEX by ∼$15 million (assuming $50/kg MOF and $10 million for additional vessels/piping) [36] [11].
### Integration with swing adsorption processes:

Among the various CO2 capture methods, Swing Adsorption Processes selectively separate CO2 and other gases close to room temperature, thus offer a more energy-efficient alternative to conventional techniques, especially for specialized industrial applications [37]. These processes have gained significant attention because the theoretical minimum energy required to recover CO₂ from flue gas and compress it to 150 bar is only about 0.75 GJ/tonne-CO2 [38].

Depending on the methods used for regeneration of adsorbents after each adsorption cycle in a CO2 capture process, several processes can be adopted to achieve CO2 separation, including (1) vacuum and pressure swing adsorption (VSA and PSA), (2) temperature swing adsorption (TSA), (3) electric swing adsorption (ESA), (4) simulated moving bed (SMB), (5) purge displacement ( Fig. 1 ) [39] and (6) Electric/magnetic induction swing adsorption (ESA/MISA) ( Fig. 4 ). In addition to these parameters, economic and technical factors must also be considered during the selection of an adsorption method. Choosing the best adsorption method for purification is thus a significant challenge [40].

CO2 recovery and purity are key parameters that determine the performance of swing adsorption processes [41]. Optimizing both is essential for achieving high process productivity, economic viability, and long-term sustainability. CO2 recovery refers to the percentage of CO2 captured from the feed gas. Higher recovery maximizes the use of the adsorbent and improves energy and process efficiency. CO2 purity indicates how free the captured CO2 is from impurities such as N2, CH4, and moisture. However, there is often a trade-off between recovery and purity. Achieving high purity can reduce recovery or increase energy demand, as more stringent separation conditions may either capture less CO2 or require additional energy for sorbent regeneration. Balancing these parameters is critical in designing efficient and cost-effective CO2 capture systems. To match the transportation/injection requirements, the CO2 recovery must be at 90% with purity higher than 95% [42]. Furthermore, a trade-off exists between the process economics and operational flexibility, which must be carefully balanced to enable the industrial adoption of CO2 capture technologies [43]. One way to introduce flexibility is by relaxing the CO2 recovery constraint; however, this can reduce overall capture efficiency. Adsorption-based processes present a promising solution, as they can be tailored to improve operational flexibility while maintaining high performance within the limits of CO2 recovery and purity [44]. CO2 recovery and purity are calculated using the following equations. (1) CO 2 Purity % = n CO 2 , EVAC n CO 2 , EVAC + n N 2 , EVAC . 100 % (2) CO 2 Recovery % = n CO 2 , EVAC n CO 2 , PRES + n N 2 , ADS . 100 % where n CO2 and n N2 are the number of moles of CO2 and N2 collected in a specific step within one cycle. Note that n CO2, PRES will be zero in the recovery calculation for the four-step cycle with feed pressurization. ADS = Adsorption; EVAC = Evacuation; PRES = Pressurization [45].

To develop efficient CO2 adsorption processes, the adsorbent should exhibit the following key properties: (1) high CO2 selectivity, (2) high CO2 adsorption capacity, (3) use of low-cost raw materials, (4) fast adsorption/desorption kinetics, (5) low heat capacity, and (6) strong thermal, mechanical and chemical stability under prolonged operations and exposure to impurities. A wide range of solid adsorbents have been designed and synthesized with controlled structures, compositions, adsorption mechanisms, and regeneration behavior to enhance CO2 capture performance [46] [47]. Among these, MOFs have emerged as one of the most promising materials in the CO2 capture field, owing to their exceptional chemical and structural tunability, high porosity, water stability, and ease of regeneration MOFs offer a versatile and efficient platform for swing adsorption processes due to their higher CO2 adsorption capacities and selectivity compared to conventional solid adsorbents. When tested at elevated pressures, MOFs have demonstrated higher CO2 adsorption capacities than commercial Zeolite 13X [48]. #### Pressure swing adsorption process (PSA):

PSA is a promising gas capture and separation technique known for its relatively low operational cost, primarily due to its use of moderate pressures. However, the mechanical energy required for the desorption step can offset some of these cost advantages. Moreover, the purity of the recovered adsorbate is often lower compared to other separation methods, which may limit its applicability in processes requiring high-purity outputs. In a PSA process, adsorbents are regenerated by reducing the pressure, making it particularly suitable for separating weakly adsorbed substances, especially when high product purity is required or when rapid cycling enhances the efficiency of the adsorbent use. Extensive computational studies on MOF synthesis and screening have been conducted to design advanced materials aimed at improving the efficiency of CO2 capture and separation processes [49]. The selection of an appropriate adsorbent for CO2 capture depends on the CO2 partial pressure and its source, such as flue gas or ambient air. For instance, zeolites can adsorb large amounts of CO2, but they typically require very low temperatures or vacuum conditions for effective desorption [50] [51] [24]. In contrast, activated carbons are promising candidates for CO2 capture, as they have already been successfully employed in PSA units for CO2 removal during hydrogen purification [52]. Despite its relatively low selectivity and density, the cost of activated carbon remains a critical factor in its evaluation as an adsorbent material. Conversely, MOFs offer high specific gas capture selectivity and capacity, along with the potential for cost-effective production. MOFs consist of inorganic metal nodes coordinated with organic linkers, forming highly tunable structures. This structural versatility allows the design of thousands of distinct frameworks with varying affinities and selectivities for CO2. The pore limiting diameter (PLD) is a critical structural parameter of an adsorbent that governs its selectivity for specific gas adsorbate. If the PLD is smaller than the kinetic diameter of the target gas molecules, those molecules are unable to enter the pores, thereby preventing adsorption [53]. The PLD of MOFs can be readily tuned through a metal substitution, providing an effective strategy to tailor their selectivity for CO2 [54]. Phase-change MOFs represent an emerging class of adsorbents for efficient gas separation, characterized by step-shaped adsorption isotherms and offering both high selectivity and capacity under optimum conditions [55]. A wide range of MOFs have been applied in PSA processes for various gas-related applications, including carbon capture, oxygen generation, hydrogen and methane storage, as well as alkane separation [56].

Grande et al. measured adsorption breakthrough curves for a quaternary gas mixture representative of the composition produced during the steam methane reforming (SMR) process using UTSA-16 MOF as adsorbent (100 g), to mimic real industrial systems [57]. This quaternary mixture is composed of 76% H2, 17% CO2, 3% CH4 and 4% CO simulating the off-gas composition from a typical steam methane reformer. UTSA-16 demonstrated higher selectivity for CO2 compared to the other gases, accompanied by significant heat generation during adsorption, as evidenced by a column temperature rise of 35 K to 55 K at low and high flow rates, respectively. Additionally, UTSA-16 exhibited a higher adsorption capacity for CO2 than zeolites, indicating its potential for use in processes involving the simultaneous production of H2 and CO2.

The previous studies revealed that the adsorption properties of MOFs are regulated by both the enthalpy of adsorption and free volume [58] [59]. Among the four MOFs studied UiO-66(Zr), UiO-66(Zr)-Br, UiO-67(Zr), and Zr–Cl2AzoBDC, UiO-66(Zr)-Br exhibited the highest enthalpies of adsorption for CO, N2, CH4, and CO2. This is attributed to its narrow pores and pore windows, as well as the presence of bromine functional groups, which enhance electrostatic and dispersion interactions, respectively ( Fig. 2 ) [60] [61]. At high pressures, UiO-67(Zr) showed the highest adsorption capacities for all four gases. This performance is likely due to its slightly shorter linkers, which create narrower pores and promote stronger dispersion interactions. Conversely, at low pressures, MOFs with smaller pore sizes, such as UiO-66(Zr) (6.6 Å) and UiO-66(Zr)-Br (6.4 Å), demonstrated the highest uptakes for CO, N2, CH4, and CO2.

Bao et al. investigated a copper-based MOF, Cu(hfipbb)(H2hfipbb)0.5, for the kinetic separation of CO2 and CH4 via a PSA process. The separation leverages the difference in diffusion rates within the MOF’s microporous channels, where CO2 diffuses more rapidly than CH4 due to its smaller kinetic diameter. This MOF exhibited a kinetic selectivity of 26, significantly outperforming conventional adsorbents in CO2/CH4 separation [62]. Mg-MOF-74 is one of the most extensively studied adsorbents for CO2 capture as it shows high adsorption performance even in the presence of moisture, due to its open metal sites located in hexagonal channels that are aligned along the c axis [63]. A computational study using a one-dimensional mathematical model evaluated the post-combustion CO2 capture performance of Mg-MOF-74 in a PSA process [64]. The model incorporated key process parameters, including pressure drop, multicomponent mass diffusion, and mass and heat transfer. A heat regeneration step was also integrated to improve CO2 sequestration efficiency. Under simulated conditions with heat regeneration, Mg-MOF-74 achieved a CO2 purity of 99.65% and a recovery rate of 69.50%. Notably, its capture performance remained stable over 10 adsorption-desorption cycles. Capturing CO₂ before combustion requires isolating CO₂ from hydrogen-heavy gas mixtures generated after the water-gas shift reaction in techniques like gasification or steam-methane reforming. Due to the elevated operational pressures and heightened CO₂ partial pressures, solvent-based absorption systems, including Selexol, are the most advanced towards commercial implementation; nevertheless, the application of solid adsorbents within pressure swing adsorption (PSA) processes is currently receiving considerable scholarly attention [65] [66]. Typical shifted syngas streams contain 15–35 vol% CO₂ and 30–80 vol% H₂ and are produced at pressures commonly exceeding 5–40 bar, conditions that strongly favor pressure-driven separation processes [67] [68]. Because PSA-based hydrogen purification is already widely deployed, pre-combustion CO₂ capture would require only minor modifications to existing processes rather than a complete redesign [69]. Moreover, the massive scale of hydrogen purification (∼50 million tonnes per year) implies that even modest efficiency gains could have substantial energy and economic impact. For instance, reducing the energy consumption of purification (7.3 MW h per tonne H₂) by 10% would save energy equivalent to shutting down 18 average-sized coal-fired power plants [69]. Consequently, the development of next-generation adsorbents for pre-combustion CO₂ capture with improved energy efficiency could significantly lower the environmental and economic costs associated with global H₂ production. Moreover, PSA is particularly advantageous under pre-combustion scenarios because the regeneration of adsorbents can be accomplished through depressurization to near-atmospheric pressure, which is energetically more favorable compared to temperature- or vacuum-swing methodologies [70]. Furthermore, CO₂/H₂ separations are inherently more advantageous than CO₂/N₂ separations, attributable to the greater disparities in polarizability and quadrupole moment, which facilitate enhanced selectivity in physisorption-based systems. In this context, metal–organic frameworks have emerged as promising next-generation adsorbents, exhibiting high CO₂ working capacities and tunable CO₂/H₂ selectivity of 5–8 mmol/g under PSA-relevant high-pressure conditions, although challenges related to stability, impurities, and large-scale deployment remain. Examples encompass magnesium based MOF-74 (Mg₂(dobdc)), nickel-based MOF-74 (Ni₂(dobdc)), copper-based Cu-BTTri MOF [BTTri= (1,3,5-tris(1H-1,2,3-triazol-5-yl)benzene (ligand))], and HKUST-1 (also referred to as Cu₃(BTC)₂), all of which feature accessible metal sites that facilitate robust CO₂ adsorption, in addition to zirconium-based UiO-66-NH₂, recognized for its hydrothermal resilience and moderate affinity for CO₂ [71] [72] [73] [74] [75]. Considering the extensive industrial utilization of Pressure Swing Adsorption (PSA) for hydrogen purification accounting for approximately 50 million tonnes of hydrogen annually even incremental enhancements in adsorbent efficacy could substantially mitigate energy expenditures and environmental repercussions [69].
#### Vacuum swing adsorption process (VSA):

A typical VSA process is commonly designed as a 4-step cycle with feed pressurization, especially in applications like CO2 capture or O2/N2 generation from air [76]. Fig. 3 depicts the four-step VSA process, which includes: (1) adsorption, (2) forward blowdown, (3) reverse evacuation, and (4) feed pressurization. VSA is appealing because it operates at lower pressures and requires less energy than alternative adsorption processes.

Farooq et al. developed a novel two-step methodology for the rapid screening of adsorbents for post-combustion CO2 capture using a four-step VSA process with light product pressurization [77]. Five key equilibrium isotherm characteristics of CO2 isotherm such as binary CO2 loading, CO2/N2 selectivity, Henry’s constant, nonlinearity at feed concentration and local slope were identified that most strongly influence process performance. By applying central composite design and neural network modeling, the authors optimized VSA cycle operating conditions for each combination of isotherm parameters. Additionally, they developed meta-models to estimate the minimum energy consumption and maximum productivity, while also assessing whether adsorbents could achieve the target performance metrics of 95% CO2 purity and 90% recovery. However, most studies primarily rely on breakthrough experiments coupled with numerical simulations to assess separation performance. This limitation stems from the practical challenges associated with scaling up MOF synthesis to the tens-of-grams scale, which is often unfeasible in typical academic settings. An additional limitation is the significant time and resource demands associated with the current VSA process. Rajendran et al. employed a hydrophobic MOF, CALF-20, for CO2 capture from a N2/CO2 gas mixture (85/15 mol%) using an integrated adsorption configuration combining basic VSA with light-product pressurization (LPP) [44]. The model predictions were validated through experimental demonstration of a VSA separation process, guided by a Pareto curve. The experiments showed that the basic four-step VSA cycle achieved 95% CO2 purity with 70% recovery, while the four-step cycle with light-product pressurization (LPP) improved performance to 95.5% purity and 88.1% recovery. Five diamine-doped MOFs, including mmen-Mn2(dobpdc) and mmen-Mg2(dobpdc), which exhibit S-shaped CO2 adsorption isotherms, were evaluated for CO2 capture using the VSA process [78]. A multi-objective genetic algorithm was employed in a comprehensive optimization and simulation study to enhance productivity and reduce parasitic energy consumption. The parasitic energy required per tonne of CO2 captured was lower for the MOFs mmen-Mn2(dobpdc) (142 kWhe) and mmen-Mg₂(dobpdc) (152 kWhe) compared to Zeolite 13X (162 kWhe). Additionally, the CO2 productivities of mmen-Mn2(dobpdc), mmen-Mg2(dobpdc), and Zeolite 13X were 0.40, 0.45, and 0.65 mol CO2 m-3 ads-1, respectively, with CO2 recovery and purity levels of ≥90% and ≥95%.
#### Temperature swing adsorption process (TSA)/ Temperature–vacuum swing adsorption (TVSA):

The TSA process involves the adsorption of CO2 molecules onto a solid adsorbent at a lower temperature, followed by desorption through heating. A typical TSA cycle consists of three main steps: (1) adsorption at ambient temperature and pressure, (2) countercurrent regeneration at elevated temperature, and (3) purging during the cooling phase. The TSA process is widely used for CO2 capture due to its readily available low- and medium-grade thermal energy resources required for complete adsorbent regeneration, as well as its compatibility with renewable energy integration [79].

In a four step TSA cycle, the CO2 capture potential of recently developed MOFs, including MOF-Mg, MOF-Mn, MOF-Co, MOF-Zn, and MOF-Fe was evaluated through a comprehensive modeling study. By optimizing the operating conditions, it was found that the energy requirement for the TSA process using these MOFs, particularly MOF-Mn, was reduced by more than 20% compared to commercial 13X zeolite. Additionally, a smaller temperature swing was sufficient to achieve comparable CO2 purity and recovery levels [80].

Among MOFs, MOF-74 is one of the most widely studied and commercially available materials, known for its high CO2 adsorption capacity and excellent CO2/N2 selectivity. These properties make it one of the most promising adsorbents for efficient and quantitative CO2 capture [81]. Ben-Mansour et al. developed a computational model integrated with Ansys-Fluent and validated both 2D and 3D CFD simulations using TSA CO2 adsorption breakthrough experiments. Under flue gas conditions, Mg-MOF-74 demonstrated a CO2 recovery of 86.5% and a purity of 96.22%. The process achieved a CO2 capture productivity of 0.279 kg of CO2 per hour per kilogram of Mg-MOF-74 and a specific energy consumption of 663.8 kWh per ton of CO2 captured [82]. MOFs generally exhibit high water uptake under normal capture conditions and water competes with other gases in the flue gas [83]. To address this issue, a techno-economic analysis of MOF-based adsorption cycles for post-combustion CO2 capture from wet flue gas was conducted to assess the cost-effectiveness of using MOFs in TSA processes [84]. The study also compares a single-stage TSA process with a two-stage system that includes flue gas drying followed by a VSA process on dry flue gas. Results showed that the two-stage process offers lower minimum capture costs for near-saturated flue gas at 25 °C and 40 °C. Additionally, indirect regeneration in the TSA system is performed by circulating a heating medium (such as steam or hot fluid) through heat exchanger tubes embedded within the adsorbent bed. Peh et al. reported that the MOF UTSA-16, operated in a five-step TSA cycle, achieved a maximum CO2 productivity of 91.8 kg CO2 m-3 MOF h-1 when treating a wet flue gas feed at 25 °C containing 15% CO2, 3% H2O (95% relative humidity), and 82% N2 [85]. A novel Mg2(dobpdc)(tetramine) MOF (Mg2MOF) was used to capture CO2 from dry flue gas (4 mol% CO2) released from natural gas power plant using a three-step steam-assisted TSA (SA-TSA) process [86]. Mg2MOF exhibited a representative double-stepped isotherm achieving more than 95% CO2 purity and over 90% CO2 recovery with the lowest specific steam consumption of 45 kg steam per kg CO2 captured and a maximum CO2 capture productivity of 0.1 molCO2m-3 adsorbents-1 under isothermal conditions. Mg₂(dobpdc)-diamine frameworks (e.g., mmen-Mg₂(dobpdc)) display step-shaped CO₂ adsorption isotherms and high working capacities at low CO₂ concentrations, making them particularly well suited for DAC [87]. For MOF-based direct air capture (DAC), hybrid temperature–vacuum swing adsorption (TVSA) is generally preferred over standalone TSA or VSA [88] [89]. Pure VSA is ineffective at atmospheric pressure because the ultra-low CO₂ concentration in air (∼400 ppm) results in negligible working capacity, even for high-affinity MOFs. TSA alone enables regeneration of amine-functionalized MOFs but suffers from long heating and cooling times and high sensible heat penalties. In contrast, TVSA combines moderate heating with vacuum, significantly lowering regeneration energy and improving working capacity for MOFs such as mmen-Mg₂(dobpdc) and PEI-impregnated MOFs, making it the most widely studied and practically viable DAC process for MOF sorbents [87] [90] [91].
#### Hybrid/integrated swing adsorption process

Pressure-vacuum swing adsorption (PVSA) operates under adiabatic conditions, where temperature changes during pressurization or evacuation can significantly affect the cyclic performance. The system’s response to pressure fluctuations is closely linked to thermal effects, which can influence adsorption efficiency and recovery. PVSA offers advantages over absorption and membrane separation processes due to its lower energy requirements, higher efficiency, and avoidance of corrosive materials. Additionally, compared to TSA, PVSA provides higher productivity and enables faster cycling, making it well-suited for large-scale gas separation applications [92].

A recent study evaluated the CO2 capture potential of a "phase-change" MOF, F4_MIL-140A(Ce), in a post combustion PVSA process [54]. F4_MIL-140A(Ce) performance is attributed to its non-hysteretic, step-shaped isotherm, low uptake for N2, and mild heat of CO2 adsorption, which was found to be better than “canonical” sorbents such as zeolite 13X, activated carbon and three MOFs (i.e. HKUST-1, CALF-20 and UTSA-16).

A modular VPSA lab-scale pilot (comprising 3 columns of 1.1 L each) was developed to evaluate 60 kg of MIL-160(Al) MOF as adsorbent for CO2 capture in various process configurations. The efficiency of MIL-160(Al) MOF for CO2 capture was assessed using a 3-bed 6-step VPSA cycle to separate 15/85 vol% CO2/N2 mixture with a pressure range of 0.1 and 2 bar. The results were compared with zeolite 13X, which showed superior performance of MIL-160(AI), achieving 92.7% CO2 recovery and 90% purity compared to zeolite 13X that yielded 85% recovery and 79.7% purity [93].

To enhance the efficiency of CO2 capture from dry flue gas, a two-stage VPSA process was implemented. The first stage employed conventional zeolite 13X, while the second stage utilized a novel MOF, Mg-MOF-74 [94]. The integrated VPSA system achieved CO2 purity of 97.57% and a recovery rate of 90.16%. Additionally, it demonstrated a productivity of 3.09 mol CO2/kgads/h, with a total energy consumption of 0.7 MJ/kgCO2. Poly vinyl alcohol binded UiO-66 (PVA@UiO-66) MOF revealed selective CO2 adsorption over N2 and the high CO2 separation efficiency in a PVSA process with feed pressure of 1.3 bar [95]. Adsorption time was found to be directly proportional to the CO2 purity that increases from 28% to 34%, while CO2 recovery decreases from 66% to 55%. The increase in adsorption time causes small N2 zone and larger CO2 rich saturation zone therefore exhibit increase in CO2 purity. The pelletization of PVA@UiO-66 MOF caused 14% drop in CO2 adsorption capacity, which was due to the increase in adsorbent’s bull density. The productivity was measured to be 0.077 kgCO2/(kgads·h), which is comparable to the experimentally studied Zeolite 13X efficiency and low operational cost.
#### Electric/magnetic induction swing adsorption (ESA/MISA)

Unlike the TSA process, the ESA process utilizes the Joule effect for low voltage electric current to directly heat the adsorbent for regeneration. As a result, it does not rely on external or waste heat for regeneration, as is typically the case in TSA [101]. Furthermore, ESA process exhibits higher tendency for volatile (i.e., low boiling) organic compounds removal than TSA.

Like TSA process, a typical ESA process has three steps: 1) adsorption at ambient temperature; 2) regeneration via temperature increase using electric heating; 3) cooling (purging) by employing treated gas or nitrogen ( Fig. 4 ). Although MOFs are generally electrical insulators, recent years have seen increasing interest in understanding and tuning their electronic structures, leading to notable improvements in electrical conductivity [102] [103]. However, there are still limited reports in the literature on the application of MOFs for CO2 capture using ESA. Kongi et al. were the first to use a redox-active MOF, Cu3(HHTP)2, as an electrosorbent for CO2 capture, demonstrating its ability to reversibly adsorb and desorb CO2 in an aqueous electrolyte through redox switching [104]. Based on the electrosorption mechanism, it was concluded that the combination of the aromatic ligand and redox-active copper centers in Cu3(HHTP)2 facilitated efficient CO2 capture. The MOF exhibited a CO2 adsorption capacity of 2 mmol/g and an enthalpy of adsorption of 20 kJ mol-1, highlighting its potential as a high-performance CO2 electrosorbent. MOFs exhibit high CO2 adsorption capacities; however, their thermally insulating nature often leads to longer regeneration times and higher energy requirements. To address this challenge, Hill et al. reported a composite of three important constituents, namely MOF (Mg-MOF-74), magnetic nanoparticles (MgFe2O4) and a hydrophobic porous polymer, which can be employed in the magnetic induction swing adsorption (MISA) process [105]. The resulting MOF composite can be regenerated after CO2 adsorption process at low energy cost of 1.29 MJ kg−1 CO2 and high productivity of 3.13 kgCO2 h−1 kgAds−1. The regeneration energy of cost of this MOF is 45% lower than the commercially used adsorbents. Thus, the MISA process has the potential to regenerate adsorbents at significantly higher speeds using a magnetic field, while offering high energy.
### Pressure swing adsorption process (PSA):

PSA is a promising gas capture and separation technique known for its relatively low operational cost, primarily due to its use of moderate pressures. However, the mechanical energy required for the desorption step can offset some of these cost advantages. Moreover, the purity of the recovered adsorbate is often lower compared to other separation methods, which may limit its applicability in processes requiring high-purity outputs. In a PSA process, adsorbents are regenerated by reducing the pressure, making it particularly suitable for separating weakly adsorbed substances, especially when high product purity is required or when rapid cycling enhances the efficiency of the adsorbent use. Extensive computational studies on MOF synthesis and screening have been conducted to design advanced materials aimed at improving the efficiency of CO2 capture and separation processes [49]. The selection of an appropriate adsorbent for CO2 capture depends on the CO2 partial pressure and its source, such as flue gas or ambient air. For instance, zeolites can adsorb large amounts of CO2, but they typically require very low temperatures or vacuum conditions for effective desorption [50] [51] [24]. In contrast, activated carbons are promising candidates for CO2 capture, as they have already been successfully employed in PSA units for CO2 removal during hydrogen purification [52]. Despite its relatively low selectivity and density, the cost of activated carbon remains a critical factor in its evaluation as an adsorbent material. Conversely, MOFs offer high specific gas capture selectivity and capacity, along with the potential for cost-effective production. MOFs consist of inorganic metal nodes coordinated with organic linkers, forming highly tunable structures. This structural versatility allows the design of thousands of distinct frameworks with varying affinities and selectivities for CO2. The pore limiting diameter (PLD) is a critical structural parameter of an adsorbent that governs its selectivity for specific gas adsorbate. If the PLD is smaller than the kinetic diameter of the target gas molecules, those molecules are unable to enter the pores, thereby preventing adsorption [53]. The PLD of MOFs can be readily tuned through a metal substitution, providing an effective strategy to tailor their selectivity for CO2 [54]. Phase-change MOFs represent an emerging class of adsorbents for efficient gas separation, characterized by step-shaped adsorption isotherms and offering both high selectivity and capacity under optimum conditions [55]. A wide range of MOFs have been applied in PSA processes for various gas-related applications, including carbon capture, oxygen generation, hydrogen and methane storage, as well as alkane separation [56].

Grande et al. measured adsorption breakthrough curves for a quaternary gas mixture representative of the composition produced during the steam methane reforming (SMR) process using UTSA-16 MOF as adsorbent (100 g), to mimic real industrial systems [57]. This quaternary mixture is composed of 76% H2, 17% CO2, 3% CH4 and 4% CO simulating the off-gas composition from a typical steam methane reformer. UTSA-16 demonstrated higher selectivity for CO2 compared to the other gases, accompanied by significant heat generation during adsorption, as evidenced by a column temperature rise of 35 K to 55 K at low and high flow rates, respectively. Additionally, UTSA-16 exhibited a higher adsorption capacity for CO2 than zeolites, indicating its potential for use in processes involving the simultaneous production of H2 and CO2.

The previous studies revealed that the adsorption properties of MOFs are regulated by both the enthalpy of adsorption and free volume [58] [59]. Among the four MOFs studied UiO-66(Zr), UiO-66(Zr)-Br, UiO-67(Zr), and Zr–Cl2AzoBDC, UiO-66(Zr)-Br exhibited the highest enthalpies of adsorption for CO, N2, CH4, and CO2. This is attributed to its narrow pores and pore windows, as well as the presence of bromine functional groups, which enhance electrostatic and dispersion interactions, respectively ( Fig. 2 ) [60] [61]. At high pressures, UiO-67(Zr) showed the highest adsorption capacities for all four gases. This performance is likely due to its slightly shorter linkers, which create narrower pores and promote stronger dispersion interactions. Conversely, at low pressures, MOFs with smaller pore sizes, such as UiO-66(Zr) (6.6 Å) and UiO-66(Zr)-Br (6.4 Å), demonstrated the highest uptakes for CO, N2, CH4, and CO2.

Bao et al. investigated a copper-based MOF, Cu(hfipbb)(H2hfipbb)0.5, for the kinetic separation of CO2 and CH4 via a PSA process. The separation leverages the difference in diffusion rates within the MOF’s microporous channels, where CO2 diffuses more rapidly than CH4 due to its smaller kinetic diameter. This MOF exhibited a kinetic selectivity of 26, significantly outperforming conventional adsorbents in CO2/CH4 separation [62]. Mg-MOF-74 is one of the most extensively studied adsorbents for CO2 capture as it shows high adsorption performance even in the presence of moisture, due to its open metal sites located in hexagonal channels that are aligned along the c axis [63]. A computational study using a one-dimensional mathematical model evaluated the post-combustion CO2 capture performance of Mg-MOF-74 in a PSA process [64]. The model incorporated key process parameters, including pressure drop, multicomponent mass diffusion, and mass and heat transfer. A heat regeneration step was also integrated to improve CO2 sequestration efficiency. Under simulated conditions with heat regeneration, Mg-MOF-74 achieved a CO2 purity of 99.65% and a recovery rate of 69.50%. Notably, its capture performance remained stable over 10 adsorption-desorption cycles. Capturing CO₂ before combustion requires isolating CO₂ from hydrogen-heavy gas mixtures generated after the water-gas shift reaction in techniques like gasification or steam-methane reforming. Due to the elevated operational pressures and heightened CO₂ partial pressures, solvent-based absorption systems, including Selexol, are the most advanced towards commercial implementation; nevertheless, the application of solid adsorbents within pressure swing adsorption (PSA) processes is currently receiving considerable scholarly attention [65] [66]. Typical shifted syngas streams contain 15–35 vol% CO₂ and 30–80 vol% H₂ and are produced at pressures commonly exceeding 5–40 bar, conditions that strongly favor pressure-driven separation processes [67] [68]. Because PSA-based hydrogen purification is already widely deployed, pre-combustion CO₂ capture would require only minor modifications to existing processes rather than a complete redesign [69]. Moreover, the massive scale of hydrogen purification (∼50 million tonnes per year) implies that even modest efficiency gains could have substantial energy and economic impact. For instance, reducing the energy consumption of purification (7.3 MW h per tonne H₂) by 10% would save energy equivalent to shutting down 18 average-sized coal-fired power plants [69]. Consequently, the development of next-generation adsorbents for pre-combustion CO₂ capture with improved energy efficiency could significantly lower the environmental and economic costs associated with global H₂ production. Moreover, PSA is particularly advantageous under pre-combustion scenarios because the regeneration of adsorbents can be accomplished through depressurization to near-atmospheric pressure, which is energetically more favorable compared to temperature- or vacuum-swing methodologies [70]. Furthermore, CO₂/H₂ separations are inherently more advantageous than CO₂/N₂ separations, attributable to the greater disparities in polarizability and quadrupole moment, which facilitate enhanced selectivity in physisorption-based systems. In this context, metal–organic frameworks have emerged as promising next-generation adsorbents, exhibiting high CO₂ working capacities and tunable CO₂/H₂ selectivity of 5–8 mmol/g under PSA-relevant high-pressure conditions, although challenges related to stability, impurities, and large-scale deployment remain. Examples encompass magnesium based MOF-74 (Mg₂(dobdc)), nickel-based MOF-74 (Ni₂(dobdc)), copper-based Cu-BTTri MOF [BTTri= (1,3,5-tris(1H-1,2,3-triazol-5-yl)benzene (ligand))], and HKUST-1 (also referred to as Cu₃(BTC)₂), all of which feature accessible metal sites that facilitate robust CO₂ adsorption, in addition to zirconium-based UiO-66-NH₂, recognized for its hydrothermal resilience and moderate affinity for CO₂ [71] [72] [73] [74] [75]. Considering the extensive industrial utilization of Pressure Swing Adsorption (PSA) for hydrogen purification accounting for approximately 50 million tonnes of hydrogen annually even incremental enhancements in adsorbent efficacy could substantially mitigate energy expenditures and environmental repercussions [69].
### Vacuum swing adsorption process (VSA):

A typical VSA process is commonly designed as a 4-step cycle with feed pressurization, especially in applications like CO2 capture or O2/N2 generation from air [76]. Fig. 3 depicts the four-step VSA process, which includes: (1) adsorption, (2) forward blowdown, (3) reverse evacuation, and (4) feed pressurization. VSA is appealing because it operates at lower pressures and requires less energy than alternative adsorption processes.

Farooq et al. developed a novel two-step methodology for the rapid screening of adsorbents for post-combustion CO2 capture using a four-step VSA process with light product pressurization [77]. Five key equilibrium isotherm characteristics of CO2 isotherm such as binary CO2 loading, CO2/N2 selectivity, Henry’s constant, nonlinearity at feed concentration and local slope were identified that most strongly influence process performance. By applying central composite design and neural network modeling, the authors optimized VSA cycle operating conditions for each combination of isotherm parameters. Additionally, they developed meta-models to estimate the minimum energy consumption and maximum productivity, while also assessing whether adsorbents could achieve the target performance metrics of 95% CO2 purity and 90% recovery. However, most studies primarily rely on breakthrough experiments coupled with numerical simulations to assess separation performance. This limitation stems from the practical challenges associated with scaling up MOF synthesis to the tens-of-grams scale, which is often unfeasible in typical academic settings. An additional limitation is the significant time and resource demands associated with the current VSA process. Rajendran et al. employed a hydrophobic MOF, CALF-20, for CO2 capture from a N2/CO2 gas mixture (85/15 mol%) using an integrated adsorption configuration combining basic VSA with light-product pressurization (LPP) [44]. The model predictions were validated through experimental demonstration of a VSA separation process, guided by a Pareto curve. The experiments showed that the basic four-step VSA cycle achieved 95% CO2 purity with 70% recovery, while the four-step cycle with light-product pressurization (LPP) improved performance to 95.5% purity and 88.1% recovery. Five diamine-doped MOFs, including mmen-Mn2(dobpdc) and mmen-Mg2(dobpdc), which exhibit S-shaped CO2 adsorption isotherms, were evaluated for CO2 capture using the VSA process [78]. A multi-objective genetic algorithm was employed in a comprehensive optimization and simulation study to enhance productivity and reduce parasitic energy consumption. The parasitic energy required per tonne of CO2 captured was lower for the MOFs mmen-Mn2(dobpdc) (142 kWhe) and mmen-Mg₂(dobpdc) (152 kWhe) compared to Zeolite 13X (162 kWhe). Additionally, the CO2 productivities of mmen-Mn2(dobpdc), mmen-Mg2(dobpdc), and Zeolite 13X were 0.40, 0.45, and 0.65 mol CO2 m-3 ads-1, respectively, with CO2 recovery and purity levels of ≥90% and ≥95%.
### Temperature swing adsorption process (TSA)/ Temperature–vacuum swing adsorption (TVSA):

The TSA process involves the adsorption of CO2 molecules onto a solid adsorbent at a lower temperature, followed by desorption through heating. A typical TSA cycle consists of three main steps: (1) adsorption at ambient temperature and pressure, (2) countercurrent regeneration at elevated temperature, and (3) purging during the cooling phase. The TSA process is widely used for CO2 capture due to its readily available low- and medium-grade thermal energy resources required for complete adsorbent regeneration, as well as its compatibility with renewable energy integration [79].

In a four step TSA cycle, the CO2 capture potential of recently developed MOFs, including MOF-Mg, MOF-Mn, MOF-Co, MOF-Zn, and MOF-Fe was evaluated through a comprehensive modeling study. By optimizing the operating conditions, it was found that the energy requirement for the TSA process using these MOFs, particularly MOF-Mn, was reduced by more than 20% compared to commercial 13X zeolite. Additionally, a smaller temperature swing was sufficient to achieve comparable CO2 purity and recovery levels [80].

Among MOFs, MOF-74 is one of the most widely studied and commercially available materials, known for its high CO2 adsorption capacity and excellent CO2/N2 selectivity. These properties make it one of the most promising adsorbents for efficient and quantitative CO2 capture [81]. Ben-Mansour et al. developed a computational model integrated with Ansys-Fluent and validated both 2D and 3D CFD simulations using TSA CO2 adsorption breakthrough experiments. Under flue gas conditions, Mg-MOF-74 demonstrated a CO2 recovery of 86.5% and a purity of 96.22%. The process achieved a CO2 capture productivity of 0.279 kg of CO2 per hour per kilogram of Mg-MOF-74 and a specific energy consumption of 663.8 kWh per ton of CO2 captured [82]. MOFs generally exhibit high water uptake under normal capture conditions and water competes with other gases in the flue gas [83]. To address this issue, a techno-economic analysis of MOF-based adsorption cycles for post-combustion CO2 capture from wet flue gas was conducted to assess the cost-effectiveness of using MOFs in TSA processes [84]. The study also compares a single-stage TSA process with a two-stage system that includes flue gas drying followed by a VSA process on dry flue gas. Results showed that the two-stage process offers lower minimum capture costs for near-saturated flue gas at 25 °C and 40 °C. Additionally, indirect regeneration in the TSA system is performed by circulating a heating medium (such as steam or hot fluid) through heat exchanger tubes embedded within the adsorbent bed. Peh et al. reported that the MOF UTSA-16, operated in a five-step TSA cycle, achieved a maximum CO2 productivity of 91.8 kg CO2 m-3 MOF h-1 when treating a wet flue gas feed at 25 °C containing 15% CO2, 3% H2O (95% relative humidity), and 82% N2 [85]. A novel Mg2(dobpdc)(tetramine) MOF (Mg2MOF) was used to capture CO2 from dry flue gas (4 mol% CO2) released from natural gas power plant using a three-step steam-assisted TSA (SA-TSA) process [86]. Mg2MOF exhibited a representative double-stepped isotherm achieving more than 95% CO2 purity and over 90% CO2 recovery with the lowest specific steam consumption of 45 kg steam per kg CO2 captured and a maximum CO2 capture productivity of 0.1 molCO2m-3 adsorbents-1 under isothermal conditions. Mg₂(dobpdc)-diamine frameworks (e.g., mmen-Mg₂(dobpdc)) display step-shaped CO₂ adsorption isotherms and high working capacities at low CO₂ concentrations, making them particularly well suited for DAC [87]. For MOF-based direct air capture (DAC), hybrid temperature–vacuum swing adsorption (TVSA) is generally preferred over standalone TSA or VSA [88] [89]. Pure VSA is ineffective at atmospheric pressure because the ultra-low CO₂ concentration in air (∼400 ppm) results in negligible working capacity, even for high-affinity MOFs. TSA alone enables regeneration of amine-functionalized MOFs but suffers from long heating and cooling times and high sensible heat penalties. In contrast, TVSA combines moderate heating with vacuum, significantly lowering regeneration energy and improving working capacity for MOFs such as mmen-Mg₂(dobpdc) and PEI-impregnated MOFs, making it the most widely studied and practically viable DAC process for MOF sorbents [87] [90] [91].
### Hybrid/integrated swing adsorption process

Pressure-vacuum swing adsorption (PVSA) operates under adiabatic conditions, where temperature changes during pressurization or evacuation can significantly affect the cyclic performance. The system’s response to pressure fluctuations is closely linked to thermal effects, which can influence adsorption efficiency and recovery. PVSA offers advantages over absorption and membrane separation processes due to its lower energy requirements, higher efficiency, and avoidance of corrosive materials. Additionally, compared to TSA, PVSA provides higher productivity and enables faster cycling, making it well-suited for large-scale gas separation applications [92].

A recent study evaluated the CO2 capture potential of a "phase-change" MOF, F4_MIL-140A(Ce), in a post combustion PVSA process [54]. F4_MIL-140A(Ce) performance is attributed to its non-hysteretic, step-shaped isotherm, low uptake for N2, and mild heat of CO2 adsorption, which was found to be better than “canonical” sorbents such as zeolite 13X, activated carbon and three MOFs (i.e. HKUST-1, CALF-20 and UTSA-16).

### TSA cycle limitations, non-isothermal effects, and the impact of powder structuring on heat transfer

Temperature swing adsorption (TSA) involves heating the adsorbent bed to desorb CO₂, then cooling to re-adsorb. Thermal inertia and low thermal conductivity of MOF beds heat flows through a material per unit thickness for a given temperature gradient create temperature fronts that propagate slowly through the bed [106] [107]. Typical TSA cycle times: 30–120 minutes (heating + cooling + purge steps) Contrast with VSA cycle times: 5–20 minutes (vacuum swing is limited by mass transfer, not heat transfer) [108]. Quantitative Example: Steam-assisted TSA with tetraamine-appended Mg₂(dobpdc) achieved maximum productivity ≈00.1 mol m-3 s-1 (moles per cubic meter per second) under near-isothermal assumptions, but required ≈45 kg steam per kg CO₂ captured in simulations, highlighting the heat penalty [109] [110]. ### Design rule 2: stability with mechanistic rationale—beyond descriptive examples

#### The problem with "Stable MOF" lists

Most reviews list "stable MOFs" (UiO-66, MIL-101, ZIF-8) without explaining why they are stable at the molecular level. This approach does not empower material scientists to design new stable MOFs, it only catalogs known examples. There is need for mechanistic understanding of degradation pathways and molecular design features that confer resistance.
#### Primary degradation mechanisms in CO₂ capture

##### Hydrolysis and ligand displacement

Water molecules attack metal-linker coordinate bonds, displacing the organic linker and collapsing the framework: M–O(linker) + H₂O → M–OH + HO–linker [119]. Weak metal-linker bonds such as Zn2+, Cu2+, Co2+ are more labile than Zr4+, Al3+, Cr3+. 4-connected nodes are more vulnerable than 8- or 12-connected nodes. Carboxylates with electron-donating groups are more susceptible to protonation and displacement. The long-term stability of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) under humid, thermal, and oxidative conditions is a critical requirement for their deployment in CO₂ capture. Several molecular design principles have emerged to enhance robustness. MOFs often degrade in the presence of water due to hydrolysis of metal–oxygen bonds. Strategies to mitigate this include: (i) High-valence, hard Lewis acid nodes: Frameworks based on Zr4+ (UiO-66), Al3+ (MIL-53, CAU-10), and Cr3+ (MIL-101) exhibit strong M–O bonds that resist hydrolysis.[120] [121]. (ii) High node connectivity: Clusters such as Zr₆O₄(OH)₄ in UiO-66 provide 12-fold connectivity, ensuring structural redundancy, multiple linkers must be cleaved before collapse occurs [122] [123] [124]. (iii) Electron-withdrawing linker substituents: Incorporating fluorinated or nitro-functionalized linkers reduces electron density on carboxylate oxygens, strengthening coordination bonds. (iv) Hydrophobic pore environments: Perfluorinated or alkyl-functionalized linkers repel water from coordination sites, further enhancing hydrolytic stability [13,14]. CALF-20 (Zn₄O(C₈H₄O₄)₃), constructed from Zn₄O nodes and naphthalenedicarboxylate linkers, demonstrates exceptional durability. When exposed to steam at 100 °C and 100% RH for over 450,000 cycles (≈10 years of operation), it retains >95% of its CO₂ capacity, with XRD confirming structural integrity. This resilience arises from moderate Zn–O bond strength combined with hydrophobic naphthalene linkers that suppress water adsorption, favoring CO₂ uptake below 40% RH [13].
##### Competitive adsorption by water

Water vapor competes with CO₂ for adsorption sites, significantly reducing working capacity: CO ₂ ads + H ₂ O g ⇌ CO ₂ g + H ₂ O ads

Water is a significant component of coal and natural gas-fueled plants flue gases, making it a powerful rival to CO2 due to its high interaction with materials containing polarizing centers. Water removal before a CO2 extraction unit would make industrial use of the process extremely unlikely, thus its presence must be carefully considered [125]. In MOFs, the presence of water can cause irreparable damage to the crystal structure by hydrolyzing the ligand-metal interactions, in addition to competitive adsorption on reactive sites [126] [127] [128]. This would limit the use of MOFs in genuine CCUS applications. MOFs exposed to humid flue gas, thermal cycling, and repetitive adsorption/desorption often undergo structural changes that reduce their CO₂ uptake. The main degradation pathways include hydrolysis of metal–ligand bonds, pore collapse, and framework rearrangement. Strategies such as water immobilization in diffusion channels have been developed to mitigate these effects [129]. Furthermore, the regeneration (temperature shift) of an adsorber that worked in the presence of moisture, generally requires temperatures higher than 373 K implying an additional energy demand with respect to an adsorber that worked in dry flow [128] [130] [131] [132]. This would limit the use of MOFs in genuine CCUS applications. For hydrophilic MOFs such as Mg-MOF-74, CO₂ uptake can decline by 50–90% at >40% RH. To address this, several design strategies has been designed [133]. (i) Hydrophobic linker functionalization: Alkyl, perfluoroalkyl, or aromatic groups reduce water affinity [134] [135] [136]. (ii) Anionic frameworks: Negatively charged frameworks (e.g., TIFSIX series) repel water electrostatically while attracting CO₂ via quadrupole interactions [137]. (iii) Pore size tuning: Ultra-micropores (<0.5 nm) exclude water while admitting CO₂, enabling kinetic separation [138]. (iv) Core–shell architectures: Hydrophobic shells (e.g., ZIF-8) protect hydrophilic cores (e.g., Mg-MOF-74) [10,139] [140]. Quantitative Example – MOF@MOF Composite: A ZIF-8@Mg-MOF-74 composite illustrates this approach. Under 15% CO₂, 60% RH, and 40 °C, bare Mg-MOF-74 exhibits 1.5 mmol CO₂/g (90% loss vs. dry), whereas the composite retains 3.2 mmol CO₂/g (30% loss). The ZIF-8 shell limits water ingress while allowing CO₂ diffusion to the core [136] [141] [142].
##### Thermal and oxidative degradation

MOFs are susceptible to structural failure under high-temperature regeneration (TSA) or in oxidative environments containing O₂ and NOₓ. These conditions can lead to organic linker decomposition through combustion or oxidation, reduction of metal nodes (e.g., Cu2+ → Cu+ → Cu0), and eventual framework collapse or amorphization. The extent of degradation depends on several factors: frameworks with weak metal–ligand bonds or volatile linkers often decompose below 200 °C, while redox-active metals such as Cu2+, Fe3+, and Co2+ are particularly vulnerable to reduction [143,144]. Additionally, aromatic linkers flanged by electron-rich substituents (amines, hydroxyl groups) are prone to oxidative attack. To mitigate these effects, molecular design strategies include the use of thermally robust linkers such as rigid aromatic dicarboxylates (terephthalate, 2,6-naphthalenedicarboxylate), incorporation of redox-inert metal nodes (Zr4+, Al3+, Mg2+), and regeneration under inert atmospheres (N₂ or CO₂) to exclude oxygen.[145] Frameworks designed via temperature-controlled synthesis, such as carbonate-pillared Zn-triazolate MOFs, demonstrate improved resilience to such chemical stress due to their reinforced structural motifs [146]. Protective coatings such as carbon or silica can further shield MOFs from oxidative species. For example, tetraamine-functionalized Mg₂(dobpdc) exhibits excellent thermal stability during TSA cycling between 40 °C and 120 °C, retaining 95% of its CO₂ capacity after 100 cycles with minimal amine leaching [147]. However, at 150 °C, C–N bond cleavage in amine groups reduces capacity by 30% after only 20 cycles, underscoring the need to limit regeneration temperatures below 140 °C to prevent degradation and maintain performance.
##### Leaching of functional groups

Functional group leaching, particularly of weakly bound species such as physisorbed amines or metal nanoparticles, is a critical degradation pathway during cyclic operation under humid or acidic conditions. To mitigate this, molecular design strategies include covalent grafting of functional groups to linkers or nodes (e.g., silane-based post-synthetic modification), coordinative anchoring to open metal sites via dative bonds (as in amine-appended MOFs), and framework incorporation, where functional groups are integrated into the organic backbone [148] [149] [150] [150]. A significant example is MOF-808-AA, consists of Zr₆ nodes and amino acids grafted to 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate linkers [151]. Under VSA cycling (15% CO₂, 60% RH, 0.1–1 bar) for 80 cycles, MOF-808-AA retained 100% CO₂ capacity with no detectable amino acid leaching by HPLC [35]. This stability corresponds to bidentate coordination between amino acids and Zr nodes, forming strong Zr–O bonds that prevent functional group loss underscoring the importance of robust anchoring for long-term performance. ### The problem with "Stable MOF" lists

Most reviews list "stable MOFs" (UiO-66, MIL-101, ZIF-8) without explaining why they are stable at the molecular level. This approach does not empower material scientists to design new stable MOFs, it only catalogs known examples. There is need for mechanistic understanding of degradation pathways and molecular design features that confer resistance.
### Primary degradation mechanisms in CO₂ capture

#### Hydrolysis and ligand displacement

Water molecules attack metal-linker coordinate bonds, displacing the organic linker and collapsing the framework: M–O(linker) + H₂O → M–OH + HO–linker [119]. Weak metal-linker bonds such as Zn2+, Cu2+, Co2+ are more labile than Zr4+, Al3+, Cr3+. 4-connected nodes are more vulnerable than 8- or 12-connected nodes. Carboxylates with electron-donating groups are more susceptible to protonation and displacement. The long-term stability of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) under humid, thermal, and oxidative conditions is a critical requirement for their deployment in CO₂ capture. Several molecular design principles have emerged to enhance robustness. MOFs often degrade in the presence of water due to hydrolysis of metal–oxygen bonds. Strategies to mitigate this include: (i) High-valence, hard Lewis acid nodes: Frameworks based on Zr4+ (UiO-66), Al3+ (MIL-53, CAU-10), and Cr3+ (MIL-101) exhibit strong M–O bonds that resist hydrolysis.[120] [121]. (ii) High node connectivity: Clusters such as Zr₆O₄(OH)₄ in UiO-66 provide 12-fold connectivity, ensuring structural redundancy, multiple linkers must be cleaved before collapse occurs [122] [123] [124]. (iii) Electron-withdrawing linker substituents: Incorporating fluorinated or nitro-functionalized linkers reduces electron density on carboxylate oxygens, strengthening coordination bonds. (iv) Hydrophobic pore environments: Perfluorinated or alkyl-functionalized linkers repel water from coordination sites, further enhancing hydrolytic stability [13,14]. CALF-20 (Zn₄O(C₈H₄O₄)₃), constructed from Zn₄O nodes and naphthalenedicarboxylate linkers, demonstrates exceptional durability. When exposed to steam at 100 °C and 100% RH for over 450,000 cycles (≈10 years of operation), it retains >95% of its CO₂ capacity, with XRD confirming structural integrity. This resilience arises from moderate Zn–O bond strength combined with hydrophobic naphthalene linkers that suppress water adsorption, favoring CO₂ uptake below 40% RH [13].
#### Competitive adsorption by water

Water vapor competes with CO₂ for adsorption sites, significantly reducing working capacity: CO ₂ ads + H ₂ O g ⇌ CO ₂ g + H ₂ O ads

Water is a significant component of coal and natural gas-fueled plants flue gases, making it a powerful rival to CO2 due to its high interaction with materials containing polarizing centers. Water removal before a CO2 extraction unit would make industrial use of the process extremely unlikely, thus its presence must be carefully considered [125]. In MOFs, the presence of water can cause irreparable damage to the crystal structure by hydrolyzing the ligand-metal interactions, in addition to competitive adsorption on reactive sites [126] [127] [128]. This would limit the use of MOFs in genuine CCUS applications. MOFs exposed to humid flue gas, thermal cycling, and repetitive adsorption/desorption often undergo structural changes that reduce their CO₂ uptake. The main degradation pathways include hydrolysis of metal–ligand bonds, pore collapse, and framework rearrangement. Strategies such as water immobilization in diffusion channels have been developed to mitigate these effects [129]. Furthermore, the regeneration (temperature shift) of an adsorber that worked in the presence of moisture, generally requires temperatures higher than 373 K implying an additional energy demand with respect to an adsorber that worked in dry flow [128] [130] [131] [132]. This would limit the use of MOFs in genuine CCUS applications. For hydrophilic MOFs such as Mg-MOF-74, CO₂ uptake can decline by 50–90% at >40% RH. To address this, several design strategies has been designed [133]. (i) Hydrophobic linker functionalization: Alkyl, perfluoroalkyl, or aromatic groups reduce water affinity [134] [135] [136]. (ii) Anionic frameworks: Negatively charged frameworks (e.g., TIFSIX series) repel water electrostatically while attracting CO₂ via quadrupole interactions [137]. (iii) Pore size tuning: Ultra-micropores (<0.5 nm) exclude water while admitting CO₂, enabling kinetic separation [138]. (iv) Core–shell architectures: Hydrophobic shells (e.g., ZIF-8) protect hydrophilic cores (e.g., Mg-MOF-74) [10,139] [140]. ### Design Rule 3: scalability beyond examples—from lab to tons

#### Scale up challenges: more than just bigger batches

Transitioning MOF synthesis from laboratory-scale grams to industrial-scale tons presents challenges that extend beyond simply increasing batch size. Key considerations include reaction engineering optimizing between batch and continuous processes, ensuring efficient mixing, heat removal, and nucleation control as well as precursor economics, where the cost and availability of metal salts and organic linkers at ton scale become critical. Environmental and operational factors such as minimizing organic solvent use and energy consumption during activation steps also play a major role. Maintaining consistent quality across large batches requires stringent control over crystal size, morphology, and defect density, while post-synthesis processing (washing, activation, storage) and shaping (pelletization, extrusion, coating) must be adapted for industrial throughput.

Reaction yield and stoichiometry are central to cost efficiency. Laboratory syntheses often prioritize crystal quality over yield, typically achieving 50–80%, whereas industrial targets exceed 90% to minimize waste. Strategies include optimizing metal-to-linker ratios, using excess of cheaper reactants (often linkers) with recycling of unreacted material, and employing continuous-flow reactors with plug-flow characteristics for precise stoichiometry control [12]. Precursor cost is another determinant: inexpensive salts such as Al(NO₃)₃, MgCl₂, and ZnCl₂ cost $1–5/kg at ton scale, while Zr(IV) salts range from $10–50/kg and rare earth or noble metals exceed $100/kg. Organic linkers vary widely, from commodity terephthalic acid ($2–3/kg, produced at millions of tons annually for PET) to specialty naphthalenedicarboxylates ($50–200/kg) and custom functionalized linkers (> $500/kg). A techno-economic analysis for MIL-120(Al) illustrates these cost drivers: AlCl₃ ($3/kg), pyridine-based linker ($15/kg), solvents ($1/kg), and energy ($2/kg) yield a total precursor and energy cost of $21/kg. With 90% yield and accounting for labor and overhead, the estimated MOF cost is $13/kg. These figures underscore the importance of using commodity precursors whenever possible and avoiding custom linkers unless they deliver at least a twofold performance improvement. Traditional MOF syntheses rely on toxic, costly organic solvents such as DMF, DEF, and THF, which require energy-intensive recovery steps. To address sustainability and cost concerns, green alternatives have emerged, including water (ideal for hydrophilic linkers and stable nodes like Al3+ and Zr4+), ethanol or methanol (less toxic and easier to recycle), mechanochemical routes (solvent-free grinding, demonstrated for ZIFs and carboxylate MOFs), and electrochemical synthesis (anodic metal dissolution in linker solution, enabling continuous operation with minimal solvent use) [159] [160]. For instance, MIL-120(Al) synthesized in water at 80 °C and 1 bar achieved 88% yield at a 5 kg scale with energy consumption of only 2 kWh/kg and 50% lower cost compared to DMF-based routes [161]. Beyond solvent choice, process design plays a critical role in scalability. Batch synthesis remains common due to its simplicity and flexibility but suffers from long cycle times (24–72 h) and batch-to-batch variability. Continuous-flow synthesis offers steady-state operation, improved heat and mass transfer, and easier scale-up, though it requires precise control of flow and nucleation to prevent clogging. Demonstrated examples include ZIF-8 production in tubular reactors with a residence time of 10 min and throughput of 1 kg/h [162]. Additionally, in-situ growth on substrates such as fibers or foams combines synthesis and shaping in one step, as shown by Mg₂(dobpdc) grown on PAN fibers in a roll-to-roll process achieving pilot-scale production of 10 kg/day [163].
#### Shaping scalability: from lab pellet press to industrial granulator

Industrial shaping methods include roller compaction, extrusion, spray granulation, and fluidized-bed granulation, enable throughputs of 50–500 kg/h, producing pellets, extrudates, or granules with controlled size distribution. Quality control focuses on particle size uniformity (±20%), porosity retention, mechanical strength (>2 MPa crush strength), and homogeneous binder distribution. For instance, CPO-27-Ni scaled via spray granulation achieved 80 kg/day throughput with particle size 300–700 μm, CO₂ capacity of 1.8 mmol/g (vs. 2.3 mmol/g powder), and crush strength of 3.2 MPa at $45/kg shaped MOF [31] [164].
#### Supply chain and manufacturing infrastructure

Barriers to scale include limited availability of specialty linkers (lead times of 6–12 months), lack of standardized MOF synthesis and shaping equipment, absence of industry-wide quality standards, storage stability issues under ambient humidity, and regulatory hurdles (REACH, TSCA registration costing $50,000–$500,000 and taking 1–2 years). Opportunities lie in leveraging existing infrastructure for zeolite and activated carbon production, ongoing ISO/ASTM standardization efforts, and contract manufacturing services offered by companies such as BASF, MOF Technologies, and NuMat.

### Design rule 4: energy efficiency in application context—matching MOF to process

Post-Combustion Capture: Flue gas streams typically comprise 4-15% CO₂ at near-ambient pressure and high humidity (60-100% RH), with SOₓ and NOₓ contaminants. To reduce heating costs, VSA or PVSA methods are preferred, however TSA can be used if waste heat is available. Optimal MOFs have moderate adsorption enthalpies (−30 to −50 kJ/mol), good CO₂/N₂ selectivity (>50), steep isotherms at low pressure, quick uptake (<5 min), and water stability (>90% capacity retention at 60% RH). UTSA-16 retains 70% of its CO2 separation capacity after admitting water into a test flow designed to imitate emissions from a real post combustion carbon capture operation. This amine-free substance is unique in its great stability (tested for 160 cycles) and minimal temperature variation [165].

Pre-Combustion Capture: Early MOF research concentrated on post-combustion flue gas circumstances. However, current studies show that for pre-combustion CO₂ capture (high-pressure CO₂/H₂ or syngas streams), a tailored "adsorbent ↔ process" match is necessary. MOFs should have high CO₂ capacity at multiple bar pressures, good CO₂/H₂ selectivity, reversible adsorption/desorption under cyclic PSA or VSA, and robustness under realistic conditions. MOF USO-2Ni outperforms activated carbon in PSA-based pre-combustion CO₂/H₂ separation, with superior cyclic working capacity at pressures up to 150 bar. Computational screening experiments show that a rising number of MOFs have attractive adsorption isotherms, CO₂/H₂ separation potential, and working capacity for pre-combustion. However, evaluations caution that many MOF investigations continue to report unrealistic equilibrium uptake.

Direct Air capture Ambient air contains only 400 ppm CO₂, necessitating high binding (−50 to −80 kJ/mol) and chemisorption for effective absorption. TVSA is typically used for regeneration at temperatures below 120 °C and pressures between 0.1-0.3 bars. Mg₂(dobpdc) with appended amines is a promising solid sorbent for DAC due to its strong CO₂ binding (isosteric heats in the range of -50 to -90 kJ/mol) and step-shaped isotherms, allowing for significant uptake even at atmospheric CO₂ partial pressures. Tetraamine-functionalized Mg₂(dobpdc) has 1.7 mmol/g capacity at 400 ppm and 50% RH, with a reduced energy cost of 4.5 MJ/kg CO₂ compared to amine-based DAC systems [166]. The decision tree provides a systematic framework for selecting appropriate processes to achieve efficient CO₂ capture in post-combustion and DAC systems ( Fig. 5 ).

Parasitic Energy Components and Heat Integration

Energy consumption in adsorption-based CO₂ capture arises from four main components: (i) compression for PSA/VSA, (ii) heating for TSA/TVSA, (iii) cooling for TSA/TVSA, and (iv) auxiliary loads (fans, pumps, controls), which typically account for 10–20% of total energy. Heating and cooling dominate TSA and TVSA energy penalties, scaling with adsorbent mass and temperature swing. Heat recovery strategies are essential to improve energy efficiency. Recuperative heat exchange between hot and cold beds can recover 50–80% of sensible heat. In moving-bed TSA systems, desorbed CO₂ can generate low-pressure steam for other uses, while thermal energy storage using phase-change materials enables inter-cycle heat reuse [11]. Although heat exchangers add $5–10 million to a 1 Mt CO₂/year plant, the energy savings typically pay back within 2–3 years [11] [167].
### Economic viability

Reducing the cost of MOF production requires a multi-pronged strategy targeting precursor selection, synthesis optimization, solvent and energy efficiency, and scale-up economies. Precursor costs often constitute 40–60% of total material cost, making the use of commodity linkers such as terephthalic or trimesic acid and inexpensive metals (Al, Mg, Zn, Zr) a practical route to reduce costs compared to rare or noble metals[168] [169]. Optimizing synthesis by increasing yields (>95%), accelerating kinetics, adopting continuous-flow or suspension-based reactors, and replacing DMF/DEF solvents with water or ethanol can reduce costs by 30–50% while enabling 50–80% savings in solvent use via recovery and recycling [170] [171]. Scale-up further reduces labor and overhead costs through automation and equipment amortization; pilot- and demonstration-scale syntheses of Al-based MOFs (MIL-120(Al), MIL-160(Al)) suggest production costs can drop from laboratory-scale values (∼$100/kg) to tens of dollars per kilogram at multi-ton scales, with forward-looking estimates projecting potential commercial-scale costs below $10/kg over 10–15 years [170]. Life cycle assessments (LCAs) remain limited, but preliminary studies indicate that the embodied energy of MOF synthesis (50–200 MJ/kg) is generally higher than that of activated carbon (10–50 MJ/kg) and comparable to zeolites (30–100 MJ/kg), though operational energy dominates the 20-year lifecycle, accounting for 90–95% of total energy use. End-of-life strategies, including recycling via metal recovery, linker reuse, or regeneration, remain largely at the laboratory stage. These studies underscore that reducing production cost and environmental impact simultaneously requires integrated optimization of chemistry, process design, and scale-up, alongside the development of standardized LCA methodologies consistent with ISO 14040/14044 [172] [173] [171].

#### Case study — design rule validation

The MOFs UiO-66-NH₂, CALF-20, and CPO-27-Ni were chosen for their unique structural and functional features, including stability, adsorption behavior, and functional group chemistry. These criteria ensure that the suggested design rules are validated for a representative set of materials.

Uio-66-NH 2 : UiO-66-NH₂, a zirconium-based MOF functionalized with amino groups, was synthesized via a rapid sonochemical method, achieving crystalline MOF formation within approximately one hour while minimizing solvent use [181]. The synthesized material exhibited a CO₂ adsorption capacity of ∼3.2 mmol/g at 1 bar and room temperature, demonstrating competitive uptake for post-combustion CO₂ capture. Experimental efforts have shown that defect engineering and ionic-liquid encapsulation can further improve CO₂ capacity and selectivity of UiO-66-NH₂, and amine-functionalized variants demonstrate significant CO₂ uptake enhancements with retained structural stability [182] [183]. Structural characterization confirmed excellent hydrothermal and chemical stability, with the framework retaining crystallinity and adsorption capacity after multiple adsorption–desorption cycles, highlighting robustness under humid conditions. Selectivity studies indicated preferential CO₂ over N₂ adsorption, emphasizing the material’s practical relevance for flue gas separations. The sonochemical synthesis approach provides a potentially scalable and efficient production route, addressing considerations of material cost and manufacturability. However, while the material’s strong adsorption sites support productivity, they also suggest that regeneration energy could be substantial, and the study did not provide full cycle-level energy or techno-economic analyses. Overall, UiO-66-NH₂ validates Rule 2 (Stability) fully, partially supports Rule 1 (Productivity) and Rule 3 (Scalability/Cost), but requires further investigation to assess Rule 4 (Energy Efficiency) and Rule 5 (Economic Viability). These findings reinforce the importance of integrating material properties, process kinetics, and energy considerations when evaluating MOFs for practical CO₂ capture applications.

CALF 20: CALF-20, a hydrophobic Zn-based MOF, was evaluated for CO₂/N₂ separation using vacuum swing adsorption (VSA) under dry flue gas conditions [96]. Multi-objective optimization combined with experimental validation demonstrated that the four-step VSA cycle achieved ∼95 % CO₂ purity with ∼70 % recovery, while the Light Product Pressurization-enhanced cycle improved recovery to ∼88 % without compromising purity [45]. The material maintained stable cyclic performance, confirming Rule 2 (Stability), while its rapid uptake and desorption kinetics validated Rule 1 (Productivity) under dynamic conditions. The ability to achieve high purity and recovery in a moderate vacuum process indicates favorable energy performance, supporting Rule 4 (Energy Efficiency), and the combination of scalable synthesis and process optimization suggests potential economic viability (Rule 5). These results underscore CALF-20’s suitability for practical cyclic CO₂ capture processes and provide a strong experimental and mechanistic validation of multiple design rules.

UTSA-16: UTSA-16, a zinc-based microporous MOF with open metal sites, exhibits high CO₂ adsorption capacity (∼4.18–4.71 mmol/g at 298 K and 1 bar) and strong CO₂/N₂ selectivity under relevant conditions, driven by accessible adsorption sites and favorable pore structure[184] [185]. Dynamic competitive tests show that UTSA-16 retains ∼70% of its CO₂ separation capacity after exposure to humid flows, with full water desorption at room temperature and robust stability over extended cycling, positioning it among MOFs with promising moisture tolerance [165]. Techno-economic analyses of VSA processes benchmarking UTSA-16 against benchmark adsorbents report higher CO₂ avoided costs (∼105–65 € t-1) compared with zeolite 13X and MEA absorption, primarily due to adsorbent cost and productivity effects, underscoring the importance of material cost and cycle performance in overall process economics [186]. Furthermore, recent continuous-flow synthesis routes achieving production rates of ∼173 g h-1 with CO₂ capacities up to ∼3.8 mmol g-1 demonstrate progress toward scalable manufacture [187]. Techno-economic analysis of MOF based adsorption cycles for postcombustion CO₂ capture from wet flue gas demonstrates that temperature swing adsorption costs are highly sensitive to cycle configuration and working capacity, with multi-stage process designs showing lower minimum capture costs and illustrating how cycle inefficiencies drive CAPEX/OPEX [84]. Collectively, these findings validate Rule 1 (Productivity) through high capacity and maintained performance under humidity, Rule 2 (Stability) via reversible water desorption and cycling resistance, and Rule 3 (Scalability/Cost) through scalable synthesis pathways, while techno-economic comparisons highlight challenges in Rule 5 (Economic Viability) that must be mitigated for competitive deployment.

CPO-27-Ni: CPO-27-Ni (Mg₂(dobdc)) was evaluated in a continuous moving bed TSA under 13.5% CO₂ at 50°C (dry). The cycle included adsorption (45 min), regeneration at 140°C (35 min), and cooling (20 min) [31]. Although equilibrium and working capacities exceeded zeolite 13X (4.2 vs. 3.8 mmol/g; 2.8 vs. 2.5 mmol/g), productivity was 0.35 mol CO₂/kg·h, 17% lower than zeolite. Mechanical stress in the moving bed caused higher attrition (12% vs. 8%), requiring 25 wt% binder and further reducing capacity. Slow heat transfer and long regeneration limited performance, and high material cost ($35/kg, 8.75× higher than zeolite) rendered the process economically uncompetitive, highlighting the critical importance of mechanical stability (Rule 2), process integration, and productivity optimization (Rule 1).

Key Insights: Productivity (Rule 1) dominates CAPEX and capture cost, while stability (Rule 2) and scalable synthesis (Rule 3) are necessary but insufficient when cycle times are long or mechanical constraints are present. Effective evaluation of MOF adsorbents requires consideration of intrinsic properties, pelletization effects, heat/mass transfer, and overall process design.
#### Mechanism validation: necessity, sufficiency, and alternative pathways

To rigorously validate the mechanisms underlying the five design rules, we examined necessity, sufficiency, and alternative pathways for material performance and process selection.

Necessity: Experimental evidence shows that specific factors are required for observed performance. For example, heat transfer limitations in Tetraamine-Mg₂(dobpdc) TSA cycles account for 68% of the 95-minute cycle time, and improving thermal conductivity in MOF beds increases cycle productivity. Similarly, MOF water stability is necessary to maintain CO₂ uptake under humid conditions, as demonstrated by CALF-20 and UTSA-16 retaining capacity under moderate RH.

Sufficiency: Mechanism-specific studies demonstrate that individual factors alone can explain performance. UTSA-16 retains ∼70% CO₂ capacity under water exposure independent of other variables, confirming that water stability alone predicts separation efficiency in competitive flue gas conditions. Likewise, improvements in pellet design and binder optimization enhance mechanical stability and process integration, sufficient to improve productivity and maintain attrition resistance.

Alternative Pathways: Comparative studies of MOFs and sorbents indicate that while mass transfer resistance, binder content, and cycle design influence outcomes, the primary determinants of productivity, energy efficiency, and cost remain thermal transport and adsorption energetics [188] [86]. This rules out alternative mechanisms as the dominant explanation for observed trends, strengthening the mechanistic framework for the five design rules.

### Conclusion

MOFs remain highly promising for CO₂ capture because of their large surface areas, tunable chemistry, and structural versatility. The comparative review materials such as Mg-MOF-74, Cu-BTC, and UiO-based frameworks illustrate the wide design space and the ability to tailor adsorption properties. Notwithstanding, the transference of empirical accomplishments to pragmatic industrial contexts persistently encounters a multitude of obstacles. The issue of scalability constitutes a substantial obstacle, as numerous high-performance MOFs depend on solvent-intensive synthetic processes that not only elevate costs but also engender environmental concerns. Emerging trends in environmentally safe techniques, which include water-centric, mechanochemical, and constant synthesis methods, showcase hopeful paths; however, it is critical that these techniques are assessed at the pilot stage to evaluate both their reproducibility and effectiveness. Stability under realistic conditions is another critical factor. While Mg-MOF-74 and Cu-BTC are moisture-sensitive, UiO-66 and related composites show better hydrothermal durability, though sometimes at the expense of adsorption capacity. These trade-offs emphasize the need to assess materials beyond equilibrium uptake, considering long-term productivity and process compatibility. Economic viability also remains uncertain. Although modeling suggests costs could drop below $10/kg with optimized synthesis and integration, current prices and system-level costs are still high. Sustainability evaluations consistently emphasize the utilization of solvents, the production of waste, and the intensity of energy consumption as significant environmental challenges, thereby underscoring the imperative for more eco-friendly synthesis methodologies and holistic process design approaches.

This radar chart (Fig. 6 ) summarizes these diverse challenges visually by classifying Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs) according to six fundamental parameters: scalability, stability, productivity, capital expenditure, economic feasibility, and energy efficiency. Materials like Mg-MOF-74 and CPO-27-Ni excel in productivity but fall short in cost and stability, while CALF-20 shows a balanced profile that aligns with industrial requirements. This showcases the elaborate dynamics that occur between design on a molecular level and overall system considerations, acting as a practical means for steering material choices and process improvements. Ultimately, advancement will hinge upon a more profound integration of materials science with process engineering and techno-economic evaluation, transitioning MOFs from potential candidates to feasible solutions for extensive CO₂ sequestration.
